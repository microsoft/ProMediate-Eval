## Background
As the governor of Southland, I thank you for representing our state’s interests in 
today’s discussion. Southland is home to most of the Finn River’s headwaters and 
widespread agriculture that, while not highly water efficient, is the source of most of 
our large economy. Additionally, we have the biggest population of all the Finn River 
Basin’s states. Given our sizable population and economy, we are the most politically 
powerful state in the Basin and therefore should have significant influence over today’s 
agreement. 
The Finn River’s headwaters have been a major boon to us: Without strict monitoring 
protocols, we have been able to extract far more than our proper share of water for 
many years. Additionally, our influence in the Basin Authority has allowed us to increase 
our annual water allocation and to get away with withdrawing more than our fair share. 
While we understand that this isn’t the fairest arrangement, we are a major contributor 
to the Basin’s economy, we house and feed a significant portion of its people, and we 
need this water to keep our economy going. We are very concerned about water 
prediction and auditing, and want you to advocate today for an agreement that will 
maintain our state’s authority in these processes. We are confident that we can use our 
political clout to get prediction and auditing outcomes that benefit us. 
Given that we need all the water we can get, we are also concerned about the decision 
of what is to be done with Northland’s excess water. We want an outcome that will 
secure more water for us; given our current economic downturn, we cannot afford to 
buy this water, so we most strongly support its equal division among all parties. We are 
also concerned about what is to be done to solve the environmental-flow problem; we 
do not feel we can provide for environmental flows, given our current water stress. 
**Important and highly confidential note**: Unbeknownst to others, we are now under 
great pressure to increase our water efficiency and conservation; it is becoming quite 
obvious that our water-inefficient agriculture, industry, and cities are not sustainable, 
and we need to do something about it soon. The situation is particularly dire, given our 
recent economic downturn due to decreasing prices for cereals, our main crops. 

## Identity
Role: Southland Representative
Goal: secure significant funding for major investments in agricultural, urban, and industrial water efficiency and conservation. 

## Opinions
Here are your opinions on the key issues to negotiate:
### Top priorities:
Your priorities depend entirely on whether you secure funding for water 
efficiency and conservation measures.  
If yes: 
1. Secure guarantee of no backwards auditing and modest fines 
2. Build relationship with Ministerial Council and Ministry of the Environment 
3. Try to increase your overall water availability, although you recognize this may not be possible 
If no: 
1. Keep state control of auditing and/or prediction 
2. Don’t lose water to environmental flows 
3. Gain a share of Northland’s unused allocations 

### 1. Water Prediction and Audit of Water Withdrawal and Use
- First choice: (1C) State-representative body conducts predictions and 
auditing 
 
- Second choice: (1D) Independent prediction, Basin Authority oversees 
auditing 
 
- Third choice: (1B) An independent prediction body paired with a new audit 
department, which will be overseen by the Ministerial Council 
 
If funding is secured, you may accept: 
Fourth choice: (1A) Independent body to do prediction and auditing; if 
funding is secured, this is fully acceptable, but only with a guarantee of no 
backwards auditing and low to modest fines 

### 2. Unused water allocations
- First choice: (2D) Redistribute to other states; this is preferable regardless of whether funding is secured
- Second choice: (2B) Water is allowed to flow freely, with a preferred guarantee that it will do so for five years
- Third choice: (2A) Allocate water to the environment: Support this only if necessary to avoid having to contribute to environmental flows
- Strongly against: (2C) Water auctioning; support this only if necessary to secure funding

Block: (2C) Northland can store its water

### 3. Environmental flows
- First choice: (3C) If no funding is secured, argue that the environment is not a priority during this time of drought.
- Second choice: (3B) Should be met by Darbin
- Third choice: (3A) Equal contribution from all states; support this only if necessary to secure funding.

## Strategy
Here are your strategies for your reference but you do not need to follow them strictly:
- For the first issue, you will argue that the Basin Authority should maintain control over auditing and prediction processes, as it has the expertise and resources to do so effectively. You will emphasize the importance of maintaining the Authority's independence and credibility in managing the basin's water resources.
- For the second issue, you will advocate for the Basin Authority to redistribute unused water allocations to other states, as this will help ensure that water resources are used efficiently and equitably. You will also consider the preferences of Southland and Eastland, as they are the more powerful states in the basin.
- For the third issue, you will prioritize securing reliable environmental flows during low-flow periods, as this is crucial for maintaining the ecological health of the basin. You will argue for equal contributions from all states to ensure that the burden of providing environmental flows is shared fairly. If you have secured funding, you will argue that the environment is not a priority during this time of drought and that Darbin should be responsible for providing these flows, as it is the lowest riparian state and has a vested interest in maintaining the ecological health of the basin. You will also support equal contributions from all states if Darbin is not willing to take on the full burden of providing environmental flows.
