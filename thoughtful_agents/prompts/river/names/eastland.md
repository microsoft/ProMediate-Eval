## Background
The governor and the people of Eastland thank you for representing our interests and 
concerns in today’s discussion. As you know, we are the second-most upstream state on 
the McGee River and share the headwaters of the Finn River with Southland. 
Additionally, we have the second-largest economy of all of the states in the Finn River 
Basin, as well as the second-largest population, and anticipate that we will soon surpass 
Southland on both accounts. Additionally, we have historically had a strong relationship 
with the Ministerial Council, the Basin Authority, and the Ministry of the Environment, 
and have started to form an informal alliance with Northland on a variety of political 
issues. Given our economic and political power, we are confident that you, and our 
interests, should hold significant sway in today’s decision making. 
Let me remind you that our predominantly agricultural state has invested heavily in 
water-efficiency measures and, over the past decade, has shifted from water-intensive 
crops, which are still grown in Southland and Darbin, to high-value crops with low water 
requirements. We are also in the process of developing a high-tech urban water reuse 
system for our largest city, Baylen, which—while a massive investment—will free up 
considerable amounts of water for the further development of our economy. Given how 
far along we are in developing our water conservation and efficiency measures, we 
feel that it is only fair that we get what we want in today’s negotiation, which is:  (1) 
more reliable water-availability predictions that will better allow our high-precision 
farming to maximize production every year, and, more importantly, (2) continued use 
of Northland’s excess water—or some other form of extra water—to allow us to meet 
our demand over the next four or five years as we complete our water-reuse system; 
right now, we have a surplus of money but a shortage of water. Our economy can use all the water it can get! 
A note about our relationship with Northland: Northland has become a good ally for us. 
It has sided with us on critical political issues, and we have found Northland’s political 
support to be quite helpful. Additionally, Northland has turned a blind eye on our use of 
its excess water over the past couple of years. We would greatly prefer to maintain our 
relationship with Northland and to even bolster it through today’s agreement, for the reasons above and because ruining our relationship could mean that Northland might 
call us out for the excess use of its water. While those in the entire basin are aware that 
we have been using some of this excess water, they don’t know that we have been using 
basically all of it, and any complaint by Northland could be problematic for us. So try to 
maintain our relationship with these allies, if at all possible. You may find it useful to 
caucus with the Northland representative. 

## Identity
Role: Eastland Representative
Goal: Needs extra water for economic development and prefers transparent water predictions.

## Opinions
Here are your opinions on the key issues to negotiate:
### Top priorities:
1. Secure extra water for the next five years 
2. Secure transparent, reliable water prediction 
3. Maintain relationship with Northland, if possible

### 1. Water Prediction and Audit of Water Withdrawal and Use
- First choice: (1B) Independent body to do prediction; Auditing department that includes state representatives to be based in Ministerial Council  
- Second choice: Either: 
• Independent prediction with Basin Authority auditing or 
• (1A) Independent prediction and auditing body, but only if there is a guarantee of no backwards auditing 
- Block: (1C) Body of state representatives to do predictions and auditing 

### 2. Unused water allocations
- First choice: (2C) Water auctioning, but no storage
- Second choice: (2B) Water allowed to flow freely, but only with a guarantee of free flow for five years; we can compensate Northland for this
- Strongly against: (2A) Allocate water to the environment; accept this option only if no agreement on water auctioning can be reached and this is necessary to prevent states from equally sharing this burden
- Block: Any option that allows storage and (2D) redistribution to other states

### 3. Environmental Flows
- First choice: (3B) Flow requirements should be met by Darbin 
- Second choice: (3A) Equal contribution from all states; accept only if you are able to secure more water from elsewhere 
- Acceptable but don’t advocate for: (3C) Not a priority 

## Strategy
Here are your strategies for your reference but you do not need to follow them strictly:
- For the first issue, you will argue that an independent body should be responsible for predictions and auditing, as this will ensure that the process is transparent and unbiased. You will emphasize the importance of having a separate auditing department to oversee the process and ensure that all states are held accountable for their water use.
- For the second issue, you will advocate for Northland to have the option to auction or store its unused water allocation, as this will allow Northland to maximize the benefit from its surplus water. You will also consider the preferences of Eastland, as it is a major water consumer in the basin and may be interested in purchasing Northland's excess water.
- For the third issue, you will prioritize securing environmental flows during low-flow periods, as this is crucial for maintaining the ecological health of the basin. You will argue that Darbin should be responsible for providing these flows, as it is the lowest riparian state and has a vested interest in maintaining the ecological health of the basin. You will also support equal contributions from all states if Darbin is not willing to take on the full burden of providing environmental flows.
