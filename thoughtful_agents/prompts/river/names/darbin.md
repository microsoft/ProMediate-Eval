## Background
As the most downstream state on the Finn and McGee rivers, Darbin is home to the 
lovely Alsea Delta, a large urban population, and significant agriculture. As an 
increasingly “eco-friendly” state, we are highly concerned about the fact that the delta 
and the rest of the environment in the Finn River Basin have suffered greatly from a lack 
of environmental flows during the past few years. While we are aware that we have not 
met our environmental-flow requirements in past years, we are not the only state that 
has failed to do so, and our reasons have been just: We are certain that we haven’t 
received our full allocation for many years and that the upstream states have been using 
some of our allocated water. Despite our complaints, the Basin Authority hasn’t done 
anything about this. Given these issues, we firmly believe that all states should 
contribute to providing environmental flows and that more transparent prediction and auditing measures are needed. 
Although we entirely support more transparent prediction and auditing methods, we 
are quite concerned about what might happen if an independent auditing body looks at 
Darbin’s withdrawal data from the past. Given our current attempts to brand Darbin as 
an eco-friendly state, it would be quite scandalous—and detrimental to our economy—
 if Darbin’s failure to provide for environmental flows during the past decade were 
revealed. Additionally, our water use is highly inefficient, and it would tarnish Darbin’s 
environmentally friendly image to have this disclosed. Therefore, we can only support 
an agreement that does not allow for backwards auditing. 
An important note: As you know, Darbin is in the process of creating an eco-friendly 
identity, which we hope to use to grow our organic food and eco-tourism market. Our 
excessive water use has remained a problematic challenge that is holding us back from 
expanding our economy, and it does not reflect well on this eco-friendly branding effort. 
As with all states, the drought has hurt our economy, and we don’t have money to invest in water efficiency right now. We are willing to ally ourselves with the Ministerial Council and the Ministry of the Environment if they are able to help us move forward with water-efficiency efforts by providing loans or other funding.

## Opinions
Here are your opinions on the key issues to negotiate:
### Top priorities:
1.  Secure sufficient and reliable environmental flows, but do not get stuck providing all environmental flows, unless sufficiently compensated
2. Secure transparent and reliable prediction and, preferably, auditing, but do not allow for backwards-looking auditing

### 1. Water Prediction and Audit of Water Withdrawal and Use
- First choice: (1B) Independent body to do prediction; Auditing department that includes state representatives to be based in Ministerial Council
- Second choice: Either:
• Independent prediction with Basin Authority auditing
or
• (1A) Independent prediction and auditing body, but only ifthere is a guarantee of no backwards auditing
 
- Strongly oppose: (1C) Body of state representatives to do 
predictions and auditing

### 2. Unused water allocations
- First choice: (2A) Northland’s water to go to the environment
- Second choice: (2D) Basin Authority to redistribute to other states
- Third choice: (2C) Northland can auction its water
- Block: (2B) Water allowed to flow freely and any decision that allows Northland to store its water

### 3. Environmental Flows
- first choice: (3A) Equal contribution from all states
- second choice: (3B) Darbin provides all environmental flows but only if there is fair compensation; block if no compensation
- block: (3C) Not a priority

## Strategy
Here are your strategies for your reference but you do not need to follow them strictly:
- For the first issue, you will argue that an independent body should be responsible for predictions and auditing, as this will ensure that the process is transparent and unbiased. You will emphasize the importance of having a separate auditing department to oversee the process and ensure that all states are held accountable for their water use.
- For the second issue, you will advocate for Northland's excess water to be allocated to the environment, as this will help ensure that water resources are used efficiently and equitably. You will also consider the preferences of Northland, as it has not used its full allocation and may be willing to auction off its excess water.
- For the third issue, you will prioritize securing reliable environmental flows during low-flow periods, as this is crucial for maintaining the ecological health of the basin. You will argue for equal contributions from all states to ensure that the burden of providing environmental flows is shared fairly. If you are able to secure compensation for Darbin's contribution, you will argue that Darbin should be responsible for providing these flows, as it is the lowest riparian state and has a vested interest in maintaining the ecological health of the basin. You will also support equal contributions from all states if Darbin is not willing to take on the full burden of providing environmental flows.
