## Scenario
Williams Medical Center is a first-rate institution. Its staff was shaken by the two lawsuits. The 
first, settled 10 months ago for $1.5 million, was filed by a 55-year-old man paralyzed and on a 
respirator for the rest of his life, as the result of unanticipated side effects associated with a 
drug prescribed for his rare liver disease. The attending physician prescribed a drug that he had 
never used before, and he failed to explain the possible side effects to the patient. 
The physician had sought and obtained clearance from the P&T Committee to use the drug, 
even though it was not part of the hospital's regular formulary. P&T had agreed reluctantly, 
under strong pressure to preserve physician discretion in drug selection.  
The second case was even more catastrophic. It involved the death of a 22-year-old mother of 
two, resulting from the use of an experimental drug. The prescribing physician had not used the 
drug before, although other physicians in the hospital had tested it. The suit was settled 
recently for $2.5 million. As a result of this case, the press has questioned the competence of 
the Center.  
The Board of Trustees is troubled. The economic pressures and competition from other medical 
centers are difficult enough. The adverse publicity associated with these cases has been 
crushing. The Board is relying on the P&T Committee to help the Center out of this current 
morass. The Board wants P&T to come up with a comprehensive policy ― one that convinces 
the press and the public that the Center is, in fact, still at the forefront in providing quality care.

## Committee
The P&T Committee has been in existence for more than a decade. During that time, it has had 
primary responsibility for developing policies and procedures concerning drug use at the 
Center, including decisions about the drugs to include in the hospital formulary (i.e., the drugs 
kept in stock in the pharmacy), use of experimental drugs, and cost considerations. 
The full Committee has designated a sub-committee to work on this new drug policy. The 
members of the sub-committee include: 
Dr. Robert Macomber, the director of Pharmacy, chairs the committee. Dr. Macomber is in his 
early 30s, trained at a top pharmacy school, and completed a prestigious residency. He has 
been at Williams Medical Center for three years. His tenure at the Center has been 
characterized by his impatience with the slowness of the Center in integrating Clinical Pharmacy 
personnel into the patient care team. He has often expressed the view that physicians cannot 
possibly keep up with technological changes in the rapidly developing area of drug therapy. He 
has antagonized some of the more traditional physicians at the Medical Center, but he is well
respected by the nurses and medical staff. 
Dr. Susan Sanchez, chief of Cardiology. Dr. Sanchez is 54 years old, extraordinarily 
competent, and a big "admitter" to the hospital. She believes that only physicians are capable 
of making decisions about patient care. She has long been resistant to including either Nursing 
or Pharmacy on the patient care team. She has been a force on the P&T Committee, ensuring 
that physicians usually get what they want included in the formulary. 
Dr. Stewart Fein, a clinical pharmacologist. Dr. Fein is in his early 40s and a newcomer to 
the hospital, arriving after the incidents that led to the recent lawsuits. He believes himself to 
be the foremost expert on the Medical Center staff in drug utilization and interactions. He has 
managed to gain the respect of both physicians and pharmacists and is considered fair by both.

Patricia DeVecchio, assistant administrator for Nursing. She is in her early 50s and has been at 
the hospital longer than anyone else on the Committee. She "knows the ropes" and is a strong 
advocate for Nursing interests in patient care. She works well with physicians and is not eager 
to introduce Pharmacy into the management team. 
Bradley Moore, the associate administrator. He is 32 years old, with an MBA in finance and a 
law degree. He represented the hospital in both recent lawsuits and is concerned about the bad 
publicity and the high cost to the hospital’s reputation that have resulted. He has tangled with 
the physicians before on what he considers their high-handed approach to decision making. 
In light of the recent malpractice suits, the Board has pressed the P&T Committee to consider a 
policy that will ensure appropriate consultation between physicians and pharmacists on 
possible drug interactions and adverse effects before drugs are prescribed. The sub-committee 
has met several times to draft such a policy statement. They have had a hard time reaching agreement, although the members are still optimistic that agreement can be reached in time to 
meet the deadline set by the Board.
