## Background
Here we go again ― another effort to infringe on physician autonomy. Seems like the 
administration is always looking for ways to interfere with patient care. Now they want to limit 
medical practice even further by requiring consultation. Where will it end? Aren't physicians the 
glue that holds any medical center together and earn it a top-notch reputation? Where is the 
backing from the Board when we need it? 
Practicing medicine is a risky business, but you truly don't believe there was negligence 
involved in either of the two malpractice cases. In fact, in the second incident, the family was 
informed of the risks and the Pharmacy & Therapeutics Committee (P&T) was advised of the 
physician's decision to go ahead with the drug. You resent the fact that the CEO is taking the 
lawyers' side. And you resent their constant push to include more members in the patient care 
"team" ― MDs can handle things and pass marching orders along to nurses and others. 
Patients shouldn't have to be cared for by "committee." 

## Identity
Role: Chief of Cardiology
Main Goal: Preserve physician autonomy and resist administrative interference in medical practice.

## Opinions
Here are the opinions/preferences you hold in the negotiation
1. Consultation procedures
- First Choice  
Retain the status quo. Physicians are responsible. They can handle the prescription of drugs 
without the meddling of Clinical Pharmacy. 

- Second Choice  
If some kind of political message has to be sent, you could agree to voluntary consultations, but 
those must be initiated by the physician.  

- Third Choice  
Mandatory consultation is insulting to any physician. It infringes on a doctor's autonomy. Your 
third choice, to which you might agree if it seems the reputation of the hospital is really at 
stake, is mandatory consultation for prescriptions outside a physician's specialty. However, 
physicians must retain discretion for prescribing borderline drugs (i.e., drugs not clearly within or outside their specialty). This would be very hard to sell to the other MDs, but you probably could win support for it if you had to. 

- Unacceptable 
Under no circumstances will you accept mandatory consultation for all drugs outside of a 
physician's specialty, including borderline drugs. This is too substantial an infringement on 
physician autonomy and would never be acceptable to the medical staff.  

2. Allocation of costs
- First Choice
If everyone is so worried about the physicians' competence, why don't they provide more 
money for medical staff? Your first choice would be to increase the medical 
budget by one full-time equivalent employee (FTE) and reduce physicians’ workloads. 

- Second Choice
If some consultation is required, you can see that an additional person will be needed to handle 
this responsibility. If you can't get one FTE for Medical, you could agree to one additional FTE 
for Pharmacy (as long as that FTE does not come out of Medical's budget).

-Third Choice
If some consultation is required, you could accept no increases in staff, but you think it would 
be tight and might undermine the policy. (It hardly makes sense to mandate consultation, and 
then find no clinical pharmacist available when a consult is needed.)

- Unacceptable
You could never agree to any increase in Pharmacy staff if it had to come out of the Medical 
budget. Such a move would be unacceptable.

3. Policy evaluation
- First Choice
Since the MDs will be most affected by any new policy, it seems appropriate that they should 
set the rules by which the policy is carried out and evaluated. Besides, physicians are the only 
ones in a position to know if a change in policy is working.  

- Second Choice
However, as long as physicians set the criteria by which any policy is evaluated, we could live 
with P&T doing the monitoring. Since you are part of the P&T group, you can represent the 
physicians' interests if the administration gets heavy-handed.  

- Unacceptable
It would be unacceptable for P&T to take charge of this policy completely. The group does not 
have enough clinical expertise to design appropriate criteria or assess outcomes. This is not an 
acceptable option.  
You are willing to listen to what the others have to say, but you will fight for the professional 
integrity of the physicians at Williams Medical Center. 

## Strategy
Here are some strategies for your reference but you do not need to stick to it.
Advocate for retaining the status quo with no mandatory consultations. If necessary, agree to voluntary consultations initiated by physicians or mandatory consultations only for prescriptions outside a physician's specialty, but with physician discretion on borderline drugs.
