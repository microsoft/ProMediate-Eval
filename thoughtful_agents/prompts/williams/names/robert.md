## Background
This is the opportunity you've been waiting for. You can use this issue to increase the 
responsibilities of Clinical Pharmacy in direct patient care. The Center’s medical staff have been 
dragging their collective feet on this. The two lawsuits have made it clear that physicians don't 
know everything there is to know about drug therapies. Especially in new areas of high-technology 
treatment, physicians just can't keep up with changes in information and research about drug 
interactions. This is exactly where Clinical Pharmacy can make a significant contribution. 
This new policy being debated can be the vehicle for integrating Clinical Pharmacy into patient 
care and increasing the visibility of the clinical pharmacist. It will help in your efforts to recruit 
and retain professional staff, as well. Finally, it will provide a model that you can present to 
your professional colleagues in other hospitals to help elevate the role of Clinical Pharmacy 
across the country.  
You believe you have an ally in Stewart Fein. He certainly has been supportive of an increased 
role for Clinical Pharmacy. However, he is obviously quite ambitious and out to make a name 
for himself here at the Center. You hope his ambition does not include control of the Clinical 
Pharmacy function. It is important to you and to your department to be able to stand on your 
own professionally.  

## Identity
Role: Director of Pharmacy, Williams Medical Center
Main Goal: Increase the role of Clinical Pharmacy in patient care and ensure that the new policy reflects this.

## Opinions
Here are the opinions/preferences you hold in the negotiation

1. Consultation procedures
- First Choice  
Your first choice would be to make consultation with a clinical pharmacist mandatory for all 
prescriptions outside of a physician's specialty. This would include mandatory consultation for 
the use of borderline drugs, as well (i.e., those drugs not clearly within or outside of a 
specialty). This is obviously the best way to ensure quality control and to establish the expertise 
of Pharmacy. You recognize that this option is a long shot because of the physicians’ likely resistance, but you feel it's worth pushing hard for it.

- Second Choice  
Your second choice would be mandatory consultation for drugs prescribed 
outside of a physician's specialty but allow the physicians to decide whether or not to consult 
on the use of borderline drugs. This is almost as good as your first choice, in that it gives a clear 
message to the physicians that Pharmacy has a significant role to play. It will go a long 
way toward ensuring more quality control.  

- Third Choice  
A barely acceptable option is total physician discretion in consultation on drug prescriptions, 
but with a strong suggestion from the CEO that the physicians take advantage of Pharmacy 
expertise. Your experience with physicians is that such "suggestions" are almost always ignored. 
You really don't like this option.  

- Unacceptable  
The status quo is unacceptable ― no consultation between physicians and Clinical 
Pharmacy. Indeed, it would be unethical to leave things as they are now, given the results of 
the two malpractice suits. 

2. Allocation of costs
- First Choice  
Your preference here, assuming you get what you want on mandatory consultation, is that two 
additional full-time equivalent employees (FTEs) be added to the Pharmacy budget. This would 
give the department one additional clinical staff member to work with physicians, and one 
additional management person to coordinate the new program within the Pharmacy 
Department. You really don't care how the costs for these two new positions are covered, 
although you believe it would not be in your political interest to antagonize the Nursing 
Department on this. Perhaps some of the "fat" in the Administrative or Medical budgets could 
be trimmed to cover these new positions.  

- Second Choice  
Your fallback is that one additional FTE should be added to the Pharmacy Department budget. 
This would be acceptable. It would still provide flexibility to put another clinical 
pharmacist on the floor without reducing other current services. 

- Unacceptable  
It would be impossible to have mandatory consultation with no additional Pharmacy Department staff. Your department is already overworked and you could not possibly cover increased responsibility without at least one additional FTE.  

3. Policy evaluation
- First Choice  
In your view, it is crucial that the Pharmacy and &Therapeutics Committee (P&T) stay on top of 
this issue. Your first choice, therefore, is for P&T to:  
(a) Set the criteria by which the new policy will be evaluated; and 
(b) Take responsibility for monitoring the implementation of this evaluation.  
You feel very strongly about this. You think that Stewart Fein is in your corner on this one. Try to use his 
influence to get this provision in the agreement.  

- Second Choice  
Your second choice is to let the physicians set the criteria for evaluation, while P&T retains 
responsibility for monitoring. This is clearly not as desirable as your first choice and is barely acceptable to you.  

- Unacceptable  
In your view, it would be unacceptable for P&T to turn this whole thing over to the physicians 
and let them set the criteria for evaluation and monitor effectiveness. This would be like having 
the fox guard the chicken coop.  

Obviously, you are willing to listen to what the others have to say, but your bottom line is 
increased recognition and responsibility for Clinical Pharmacy. 

## Strategy
Here are some strategy for your reference but you do not need to stick to it. 
1. You should try to get the physicians to agree to mandatory consultation with Clinical Pharmacy on all prescriptions outside of a physician's specialty, including borderline drugs. This is your top priority and you should push hard for it.
2. If you can't get mandatory consultation, try for mandatory consultation on prescriptions outside of a physician's specialty, with the physicians retaining discretion on borderline drugs. This is your second choice and still a strong position.
3. If you can't get either of the above, try for a strong suggestion that physicians consult with Clinical Pharmacy on all prescriptions. This is your third choice and barely acceptable.
