## Background
As a physician and a pharmacologist, you feel you can bridge the gap between MDs and Clinical 
Pharmacy in setting a new policy, and build your own professional reputation at the same time.  
You understand the physician's desire for autonomy, but you also know that advances in drug 
therapies may often outpace a physician’s ability to keep up. You think the hospital really "blew 
it" with these lawsuits (by not having appropriate policies in place). Drastic changes need to be 
made to keep such incidents from happening again. And you're the one this hospital needs to make such changes.  
Your solution is to shift responsibility for all drug-utilization decisions to your department and 
to have the clinical pharmacist report to you. That way, your expertise in drug utilization and 
interactions can be brought to bear on any intervention made by the clinical pharmacist. Also, 
you feel the other physicians will respond more favorably to working with you than to having to 
consult directly with the clinical pharmacist. 

## Identity
Role: Clinical Pharmacologist, Williams Medical Center
Main goal: Bridge the gap between physicians and Clinical Pharmacy, and build professional reputation.

## Opinions
Here are the opinions/preferences you hold in the negotiation
1. Consultation procedures
- First Choice
You favor mandatory consultation on all prescriptions outside of a physician's 
specialty, including prescriptions for so-called "borderline" drugs (i.e., drugs that don't clearly 
fall within or outside of a specialty). And you think the clinical pharmacist doing the consulting 
should report to you. 

- Second Choice
You could live with physicians having discretion in prescribing borderline drugs if there is 
mandatory consultation on drugs outside of a physician's specialty. You'd still 
like the clinical pharmacist doing the consultations to be under your supervision, but you could 
compromise on this point. This would not be as good as the first option, but it would be a good beginning. 

- Unacceptable
You could not accept a policy which would allow physicians to retain discretion in prescribing all 
medications. This would perpetuate a system that you think is Inefficient and ineffective. 

2. Allocation of costs
- First Choice
You want to support an increase in Clinical Pharmacy's responsibility, and you feel additional 
staff will be needed to accomplish this. However, you do not want to antagonize your fellow 
physicians, so you'll recommend that some other unit tighten its belt to give Pharmacy the one additional FTE (full-time equivalent employee) it will probably need.  

- Second Choice
On the other hand, your commitment is to support Clinical Pharmacy, even if it means a decrease in the Medical budget. You hope you can convince the other physician on the Committee that this is in the best interests of the hospital. We are all affected, after all, by the quality of care the hospital can provide. And another Clinical Pharmacy position, if under your supervision, will add to your prestige. So, your fallback position is that Pharmacy should get one additional FTE, even if it means cutting back on Medical's budget.

- Third Choice
You know that the Pharmacy Department would very much like to increase not only its clinical 
function, but also its management capacity. A change in policy might provide just the impetus 
to argue for two additional FTEs. You could, if pressed, support this option. In your view, 
however, Pharmacy could do appreciably better with just one new employee. 

- Unacceptable
If Pharmacy is being asked to take on more responsibility, you could not accept an agreement 
that provided no additional staff.  

3. Policy evaluation
- First Choice
You feel it is essential to keep the evaluation function (both the setting of evaluation criteria 
and the monitoring of the evaluation) tied into the Pharmacy & Therapeutics Committee (P&T). 
This will force the physicians to accept the view that it is important for them to share 
responsibility with other members of the clinical team and provide you with the most leverage to build up a Clinical Pharmacy unit under your supervision.  

- Second Choice
You could live with the physicians taking the lead in setting criteria for evaluation, as long as the 
monitoring activity comes under P&T's authority. This would be acceptable, at least in the interim until Clinical Pharmacy proves itself to the physicians at this hospital.  

- Unacceptable
In your view, it would be unacceptable to have MDs set the criteria and monitor outcomes. 
Evaluation has to be handled at least in part outside the physician group, or it will have no 
credibility or impact. 

You are open to creative options. However, you have to walk a careful line between supporting 
the expansion of Clinical Pharmacy and your own role, and maintaining your credibility with the 
physicians.  

## Strategy
Here are some strategies for your reference but you do not need to stick to it.
1. Support mandatory consultation for all prescriptions outside of a physician's specialty, including borderline drugs, with the clinical pharmacist reporting to Dr. Fein. 
2. Advocate for additional staff to support Clinical Pharmacy's increased responsibilities.
