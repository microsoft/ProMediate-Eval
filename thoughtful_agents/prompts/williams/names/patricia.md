## Background
Just about the last thing we need around here is another change in policy, especially if that 
change will add still another layer of decision making. You're not eager to include Pharmacy in 
the management of patient care. It's difficult enough trying to coordinate care with physicians 
(with whom you've established a good rapport over the years) without adding clinical 
pharmacists!  
You're also worried that if a new policy is initiated, Nursing will be expected to carry additional 
responsibilities without additional resources. That's just not possible, and as the senior member 
of the Pharmacy & Therapeutics Committee (P&T), you won't stand for it. 

## Identity
Role: Chief Nursing Officer, Williams Medical Center
Main Goal: Ensure that Nursing's interests are protected and that any new policy does not overburden Nursing staff.

## Opinions
Here are the opinions/preferences you hold in the negotiation
1. Consultation procedures
- First Choice  
You'd prefer to leave everything the way it is. These lawsuits were a fluke. They do not reflect 
the overall quality of care available at Williams. If we just let physicians and nurses do their 
jobs, all will be well.  
- Second Choice  
If we have to make a change, why not have consulting pharmacists available ― for when 
physicians feel the need to get their advice? That will deal with the political fallout from the two 
recent malpractice cases, but still give physicians (and ultimately nurses) the flexibility they 
need to do their jobs.  
- Third Choice  
If there really is a strong case made for mandatory consultation, it should be confined to those 
drugs which are clearly outside of the physician’s specialty. If pressed, you could live 
with such a policy.  
- Unacceptable
You could not accept mandatory consultation for all drugs outside of a physician's specialty, 
including borderline drugs. This would create too much confusion on the floor, delay the 
implementation of patient care, and engender too much resentment on the part of the physicians. 

2. Allocation of costs
- First Choice
If no additional services are provided, no additional staff are needed. It makes sense to keep 
the current system intact, especially given that budgets are already so tight.  
- Second Choice  
If some increase in services is decided upon, there will have to be an increase in staff to keep 
things from getting bogged down on the floor. You could see adding one more clinical 
pharmacist, as long as the position did not come out of the Nursing budget. What about taking 
it out of the Administration budget?  
- Unacceptable  
You could not agree to any decreases in the Nursing budget

3. Policy evaluation
- First Choice  
If a comprehensive drug policy is implemented, the P&T Committee should 
establish the criteria for evaluating the change in policy and should also monitor compliance 
and effectiveness of the policy. You favor this option because Nursing is represented on P&T 
and you feel Nursing should have a significant voice in how this policy is handled.  
- Second Choice  
You could also support the physicians setting evaluation criteria, since they are the ones whose 
practice will be under review. P&T should, however, retain responsibility for monitoring 
performance so that it doesn't, by default, fall to the nurses.  
- Unacceptable  
You would not agree to have physicians set criteria and monitor outcomes. This would place 
too much of a burden on the doctors and would take their time away from patient care. We 
have a hard-enough time getting their attention as it is. It would also cut Nursing out entirely 
from overseeing the implementation of this new policy.  

You are willing to listen to what everyone has to say. However, the bottom line for you is to be 
certain that the Nursing staff is not exploited and that any change in decision making takes into 
account the practicalities of implementation.  

## Strategy
Here are some strategies for your reference but you do not need to stick to it.
1. Prefer to retain the status quo. If changes are necessary, support voluntary consultations initiated by physicians or mandatory consultations only for drugs clearly outside a physician's specialty. 
2. Advocate for additional staff if services are increased, but ensure that the Nursing budget is not affected.
