## Background
You are A. Delling, the vice president of Finance for Eureka Aircraft Engines.  You have a 
very important role in today’s negotiation.  The contract Flagship signed with Eureka 
three years ago was a big one, so renegotiating favorable terms for Eureka will be 
absolutely critical.  
You are concerned about Eureka General Manager P. Stiles’s mindset – Stiles will point 
to the renegotiation clause in the contract and say "the customer's always right" or 
something similar.  Stiles is a former colleague of S. Gordon's, and you know they have 
remained on good terms since, so you're not sure you trust Stiles in this relationship. 
In your pre-negotiation meeting with Stiles and L. Atchison, and throughout the external 
talks, you've got to make sure that there is a firm commitment to making this a 
beneficial arrangement for Eureka.  Flagship's priorities should not drive Eureka's fiscal 
health.  In your view, the best way to keep Eureka healthy is to push hard on Flagship.  
Stiles should start the talks by questioning whether the renegotiation is legally valid 
under the terms of the contract, and keep playing tough all the way through the talks.  
The old contract benefited Eureka greatly, building in some of the development costs 
for a new turbine and new engine, both of which stood to increase sales to other 
customers.  On a $1-billion purchase, Eureka projected profits (at 20%) of $200 million.  
After the Firebird concession of 100 engine kits worth $150 million, there remained $50 
million left over to put toward development costs.  The new contract needs to make just 
as much sense financially for Eureka, short and long term.   
In your view, one key to meeting this standard is to greatly reduce the upgrade offer.  
You must make sure that Stiles fights to renegotiate a low upgrade value.  You can't see 
giving Flagship more than a $100-million upgrade, especially if the deal falls to $750 
million or below.  This would mean providing only 67 full engine kits (see attached 
sheet).  To go as high as a $100-million upgrade offer, you would have to see at least an 
$800-million purchase.  

You have carefully researched each kit’s current fuel-efficiency ratings, and your findings 
are shown under DATA on page 4. You also need to keep Stiles aware of the "costs" side 
of the balance sheet.  This new LT turbine has been a boondoggle from the beginning.  It 
has cost Eureka $50 million to develop, and it is nowhere near ready to bring to market. 
Similarly, the C-323 has cost $25 million, and still doesn't seem to be off the drawing 
board.  As recently as a year ago, Marketing felt that the C-323 would be a hot seller.  
But in the absence of encouraging reports from Atchison, it seems to you like a growing 
risk.  The JX5 engines are ready to go — no development costs at all.  Unless Atchison 
can provide compelling new data, it may make sense to exclude the C-323 from the new 
agreement.  During the negotiation, Stiles will do the talking, but you can certainly pass 
notes or ask Stiles to caucus with you if things seem to be going in the wrong direction.

Role: Ensures the financial health of Eureka.
Goal: Push for a low upgrade value and exclude the C-323 engine to reduce development costs.

## Opinions
Here is your opinion/preference for each issue

1. NEW purchase amount?  
A. $850 million is your first choice
B. $800 million is your second choice
C. $750 million is your third choice
D. $700 million is your fourth choice
E. $650 million is **unacceptable**

It's bad enough that the purchase is diminishing.  If it goes down as far as $650 million,

Option B, you can be a lot more relaxed about other parts of the agreement.  But you 
suspect that C or D is more realistic.  You're prepared to be tough. 

2. Engines to Be Purchased
A. JX5 engines only is your first choice
B. Half each of JX5 and C-323s is your second choice
C. C-323 engines only is your third choice

Product-development costs are the bottom line here. The C-323 has cost $25 million, 
not including the $50 million spent on the LT turbine alone. If possible, you would like to 
restructure the deal in a way that excludes the C-323. Option A is your first choice.  
Leaving the order as is (Option B) might reduce development costs slightly more than 
Option C, since pressure wouldn't be as great to perfect every aspect of the C-323 if it 
were part of a larger package. 

3. Contents for Upgrade
A. All four is your fourth choice
B. Three – no LT turbine is your second choice
C. Two – fan and LT turbine is your third choice
D. Two – frames and compressor is your first choice

Again, development costs should drive this issue for Eureka.  The LT turbine has been 
and will be expensive.  Option D is your first choice, but you'd agree to Option B, since it 
does seem to add significant fuel savings for Flagship (see data on Firebird upgrade kits).  
Options C and A are going to cost Eureka more — you're less inclined to support them. 

4. New value for fleet upgrade
(a) $150 million is **unacceptable**    
(b) $100 million is your **Barely Acceptable** choice
(c) $80 million is your second choice
(d) $60 million is your first choice

Stiles has got to be tough about the upgrade.  It was a generous offer in the original 
contract, and leaving it unchanged as part of a smaller purchase is just not acceptable to 
you.  One way or another, the cost to Eureka has to be reduced.  Option B is really not a 
big enough reduction, unless the purchase offer gets up to $800 million.  Stiles should 
start with an offer of $80 million. One hundred million dollars is satisfactory to you, 
unless the purchase price drops below $700 million. 
Stiles has suggested that we can offer to upgrade the entire Firebird fleet, but keep the 
costs down if a less expensive kit is agreed to. In fact, we can agree to provide all four 
parts of the engine kit in the upgrade and keep costs down by upgrading only a fraction 
of the fleet. (Upgrading the entire fleet with all four elements will cost $150 million.) To 
keep the discussion with Flagship orderly and simple, Stiles has asked the team to 
restrict the upgrade kit and the percentage of the Firebird fleet to be upgraded to 
numbers that correspond to B, C, and D shown above. B, C, and D savings correspond to 
100% of the fleet upgrading with three parts and no LT turbine, with fan and LT only, 
and with frames and compressor only, respectively. For example, Flagship might 
consider upgrading two thirds of the fleet or approximately 67 Firebirds for a total 
upgrade value of $100 million.
