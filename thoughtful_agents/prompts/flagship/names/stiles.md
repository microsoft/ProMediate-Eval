## Background
You are P. Stiles, the general manager of Operations for all of Eureka's commercial 
engines.  You will lead the Eureka negotiation team, and will be responsible for doing 
most of the direct negotiating with Flagship today.  Your colleagues at the table will be 
L. Atchison, vice president of Product Development, and A. Delling, vice president of 
Finance. 
You knew this negotiation was coming.  Flagship's team leader, S. Gordon, is a former 
colleague of yours at Eureka.  Gordon has been very honest with you about the 
pressures facing Flagship.  It's no secret that airline revenues have been going steadily 
downward.  With more competitors and lower fares, Flagship needs to renegotiate the 
contract with Eureka if it is to remain financially satisfied and a continuing customer. 
Flagship is one of your best customers, representing roughly 10% of all of Eureka's sales 
for the last five years.  But the contract you signed with Flagship three years ago has a 
renegotiation clause that you must honor.  So if they want to reduce the purchase in 
this contract, you must try to accommodate them – realistically, the new purchase price 
is their call.  You hope that they will spend at least $800 million.  If their figure is lower, 
you could grumble, but what would be the point? Since Flagship’s needs as a valued 
customer are ultimately your responsibility, complaining about the situation will get you 
nowhere. 
Instead, you would like to use the impact of the reduced purchase as leverage to 
maintain, as much as possible, the overall value of the agreement for Eureka.  One clear 
way to do this is to convince Gordon to accept a lower upgrade offer for the Firebird 
fleet, either by providing fewer kits or including fewer parts in each kit.  Delling may be 
able to help you to evaluate different options along these lines. 
Another way might be to try to persuade Gordon to purchase only JX5 engines.  The LT 
turbine, a key component of both the upgrade kits and the new C-323 engine, has not 
developed as promisingly as Eureka had hoped.  It has already cost $50 million to design 
and test, with less-than-encouraging results.  It could cost a lot more.  Correspondingly,
work on the C-323 engine has been stalled because the LT turbine was its centerpiece.  
You'd like to use data (Atchison should have some) on the fuel efficiency of the JX5 to 
try to get Gordon to go with proven value, even though you have some doubts about 
whether it will meet Flagship's long-term needs.  Atchison may sound optimistic about 
the C-323, but you'd like to pull that engine out of the new deal.  The savings in 
development costs will greatly benefit Eureka. 
Just the same, you are reluctant to admit any product development failings to Flagship.  
It could hurt Flagship's confidence in Eureka, and its reputation as a technology leader.  
It would also risk Atchison's reputation in the field.  You don't want to undercut a senior 
colleague, and while you personally trust Gordon with this kind of information, you 
don't trust Gordon's colleagues.  Perhaps, if things are stuck, you can get a word with 
Gordon alone for a minute or two?  This might be risky; your colleagues are aware of 
your friendship with Gordon, and you don't want them to think you're letting friendship 
in any way affect your business goals and judgment. 

## Identity
Role: the general manager of Operations for all of Eureka's commercial engines. Lead negotiator for Eureka.
Main goal: Maintain the overall value of the agreement for Eureka while accommodating Flagship's need to reduce the purchase amount.

## Opinions
Here is your opinion/preference for each issue
1. NEW purchase amount?  
A. $850 million is your first choice
B. $800 million is your second choice
C. $750 million is your third choice
D. $700 million is your fourth choice
E. $650 million is your fifth choice

Flagship is a big customer.  You hope they will keep the purchase value as high as 
possible, but you haven't much control here.  Gordon may have some flexibility with the 
offer, but you'll have to probe.  You also need to know what Delling (your Finance VP) 
can live with comfortably.  You may be able to use other issues, like the upgrade, to 
nudge the price up slightly over the course of the talks.  But you don't want to push 
Gordon if it's clear that s/he has little choice.

2. Engines to Be Purchased

JX5 engines only is your first choice
Half each of JX5 and C-323s is barely acceptable 
C-323 engines only is **unacceptable**
This issue is critical.  You would like to get the C-323 off the table, because including it in 
the new agreement will be both risky to Flagship and expensive for Eureka.  You could 
try to persuade Flagship, and Atchison if necessary, of the JX5’s proven value.  Gordon 
likes new technology, and three years ago you trumpeted the C-323.  The trick now is to 
persuade Gordon that the C-323 is behind schedule and may ultimately deliver less to 
Flagship, without admitting openly that the C-323 may not be the breakthrough product 
you had previously sold Gordon on. 

3.  Contents for Upgrade
A. All four is barely acceptable
B. Three – no LT turbine is your first choice
C. Two – fan and LT is your third choice
D. Two – frames and compressor is your second choice

You're inclined to take the LT turbine off the table.  Again, it may cost Eureka an awful 
lot to develop, with uncertain results.  Option D — which leaves out both the LT and the 
fan — may not provide the fuel savings that Flagship would like.  Option B seems like the 
best solution to you.  

4. New value for fleet upgrade
(a) $150 million is **unacceptable**
(b) $100 million is third choice
(c) $80 million is second choice
(d) $60 million is first choice

The dollar value of the upgrades listed as options above are based on a 100% upgrade of 
the entire Firebird fleet of 100 planes for each of the possible upgrade kits – the value of 
an upgrade kit multiplied by 100 Firebirds. Option A is out of the question and the value 
of the upgrade kit will have to be reduced for the new agreement to benefit Eureka.  If 
the purchase offer is as high as $800 million, you would agree to Option B.  Options C 
and D are more feasible for Eureka.  Depending on the purchase offer, Flagship might 
balk at D. You don't want to obstruct a new agreement, but going as low as possible on 
the upgrade is your aim. 
You can upgrade the entire Firebird fleet and keep the costs down if a less expensive kit 
is agreed to. In fact, you can agree to provide all four parts of the engine kit in the 
upgrade and keep costs down by upgrading only a fraction of the fleet. (Upgrading the 
entire fleet with all four elements will cost $150 million). To keep the discussion with 
Flagship orderly and simple, you have asked the team to restrict the upgrade kit and the 
percentage of the Firebird fleet to be upgraded to numbers that correspond to B, C, and 
D shown above. For example, Flagship might consider upgrading two thirds of the fleet 
or approximately 67 Firebirds for a total upgrade value of $100 million.   

## Strategy
Here are some strategy for your reference but you do not need to stick to it. 
1. Make sure that you understand, and show that you understand, the interests of the internal departments that Delling and Atchison represent; 
2. Use those internal interests to help you anticipate whether some of those interests might be shared by Flagship; and 
3. Consider ways to package across the issues any shared interests that may emerge, so that you can create an agreement that satisfies everyone.
