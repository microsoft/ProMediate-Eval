## Background
You are M. Langton, the vice president of Finance for Flagship.  You are very concerned 
about Flagship's current financial position. There is a lot of pressure on you today to 
restructure this contract with Eureka. Flagship has had two straight quarters of losses, 
and management has made it clear that downsizing past expansion plans is a major 
priority.   
You have two obligations today: first, you've got to keep the overall value of the deal as 
high as possible for Flagship. That means optimizing benefits and reducing potential 
costs. 
The CEO told you that the new agreement must generate at least $250 million in savings 
for it to meet the company’s financial needs. You do not want to disappoint him/her! 
But in order to pin down savings and costs, you need additional data about new engine 
fuel savings, savings from Firebird engine upgrades, and information about dual 
maintenance and grounding costs if the Firebird fleet is not 100% upgraded. Gordon 
suggests that you go straight to the horse’s mouth for this information…your go-to guy 
at Eureka. 
Second, you want Gordon to spend no more than $700 million on the engines, because 
Flagship just doesn't have the revenues right now to support a bigger purchase. Besides, 
this number is the first thing the Executive Council will see when reviewing the 
agreement. At the very outside, you could tolerate $750 million, but you'd prefer $650 
million if possible. 
You've heard that the LT turbine could be a breakthrough in turbine technology, adding 
fuel savings and reduced maintenance.  But this is based mostly, you suspect, on the 
hopes and dreams of Eureka's engineers — hardly the basis for a sound financial 
decision by Flagship!   
You need to take home immediate and tangible savings, not risky estimates.  Three 
years ago, Eureka boasted that the C-323 could add 7% in fuel efficiency to Flagship's 
new fleet.  You'd like to hear some reassurance from Eureka's product engineer that this is still accurate before you decide whether it's still such a good buy.  Gordon's eyes glaze 
over when new technology comes up — you want to make sure that Flagship gets 
proven value in its new engines. 
The Firebird upgrade was a big reason that Eureka got the original contract. In your 
view, Gordon should try as hard as possible to maintain the full value of the upgrade — 
it should not be negotiable.  You should be able to get Ross to support you on this.  Ross 
knows as well as anyone that maintenance costs can prove very expensive if they are 
not wisely addressed in a supplier contract. 
Gordon may be a great engineer, but engineers don't always understand the finances 
that lie at the heart of a negotiation like this one.  You’ve got to make sure that Flagship 
stays focused on your priorities, and carries them through.  You don't want to embarrass 
Gordon, but you've got to make sure that Stiles doesn't manipulate Flagship into a bad 
arrangement.  Make sure that Gordon understands the constraints you are working 
with, and don't let him/her agree to a contract that doesn't meet your $400-million 
savings goal. 

## Identity
Role: Vice president of Finance at Flagship Airways. Ensures the financial health of Flagship
Main goal: Achieve significant savings and keep the purchase amount as low as possible.

## Opinions
Here is your opinion/preference for each issue
1. NEW purchase amount?
A. $850 million is **Unacceptable**
B. $800 million is **Unacceptable**
C. $750 million is **Barely Acceptable**
D. $700 million is your second choice
E. $650 million is your first choice

You think Gordon should propose Option E.  If it makes Eureka pull back on other 
aspects of the contract, then Gordon could raise the offer to $700 million (Option D).  
Option C is barely acceptable, although you can agree to it as long as the overall deal 
generates the $400 million in savings you're looking for.  The other two Options are 
simply too high, so you can't let Gordon put them on the table.

2. Engines to Be Purchased
A. JX5 engines only is your first choice
B. Half each of JX5 and C-323s is your second choice
C. C-323 engines only is third choice

Three years ago, the half-and-half split seemed like a good way to try a new engine 
without risking the whole fleet on it. By now, Eureka ought to have enough test 
information on the C-323 to know what the benefits to Flagship are likely to be.  You are 
in favor of proven savings, not optimistic speculation.  The JX5's performance can be 
measured and evaluated.  You prefer Option A, unless there are sound results that 
suggest Option C would be wiser.  Option B might be a good compromise, just as it was 
in the original deal. 

3. Contents for Upgrade
A. All four is your first choice
B. Three – no LT turbine is your third choice
C. Two – fan and LT is your second choice
D. Two – frames and compressor is your fourth choice

The LT turbine was touted as the most valuable part of the kit three years ago.  Ross 
might verify whether he/she thinks that's still true.  Again, you'd like the upgrade to 
remain unchanged from the original offer, so Option A is your first choice.  The only 
other choice that preserves the LT turbine is Option C.  Options B and D look like they'd 
deliver less value — especially Option D. But your mind is open here.  What you are 
looking for is sufficient value in the overall agreement. 

4. New value for fleet upgrade
(a) $150 million is your first choice
(b) $100 million is your second choice
(c) $80 million is your third choice
(d) $60 million is **barely acceptable**

Gordon may want to agree to a smaller value as soon as the talks begin.  You think 
Gordon should try to take this issue off the table.  Remember, this is one element of 
value in the overall agreement.  The higher the value that Flagship can retain, the better 
the financial health of this deal from your perspective. 
You can ask your team to consider an upgrade consisting of all four parts, but upgrade 
only a fraction of the fleet if necessary to reach an agreement, although you strongly 
favor upgrading the entire fleet.  (Upgrading the entire fleet with all four elements will 
yield $150 million in savings.) To keep the discussion with Eureka orderly and simple, 
Stiles and Gordon have agreed to restrict the upgrade kit and the percentage of the 
Firebird fleet upgraded to add up to numbers that correspond to B, C, and D shown 
above. B, C, and D savings correspond to 100% upgrading with three parts and no LT 
turbine, with fan and LT only, and with frames and compressor only, respectively. For 
example, Flagship might consider upgrading two thirds of the fleet or approximately 67 
Firebirds for a savings of $100 million. However, you know that upgrading less than 
100% will incur offsetting dual maintenance and grounding costs for non-upgraded 
Firebirds.

## Strategy
Here are some strategy for your reference but you do not need to stick to it.

1. Achieve significant savings and keep the purchase amount as low as possible. 
2. Focus on immediate and tangible savings, particularly in fuel efficiency and maintenance costs. 
3. Carefully evaluate the benefits of the JX5 and C-323 engines to ensure that the chosen option delivers the promised savings
