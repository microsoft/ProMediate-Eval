## Background
You are L. Atchison, the vice president of Product Development for Eureka, responsible 
for bringing new commercial engines to market.  If Flagship insists on a downsized 
agreement, you want to make sure to minimize any adverse impacts on product 
development.   
You have tried over and over again to make the point that Eureka must support long
term product development objectives in each customer contract if it is to remain a 
technology leader in the future.  The continuous pressures at Eureka toward quarterly 
profits and customer-driven agreements have made long-term quality a real challenge 
for product development.  You would like to see more recognition that long-term 
research and development objectives are essential to Eureka's continued success in the 
coming decades. 
Nevertheless, as the person overseeing development of the LT turbine and the C-323 
engine, you are in a quandary at the moment.  The deadline for delivering both the LT 
and the C-323 to Flagship is only 12 months away, and you are increasingly wary of the 
frequently revised estimates to which your engineers cling. 
The LT turbine boasts a new material and new design that your engineers initially 
claimed could increase fuel savings dramatically while reducing maintenance costs. 
Preliminary testing was indeed promising.  You had hoped that the LT would put Eureka 
firmly in the lead in turbine technology, and that it would be relatively simple to build a 
new engine around the LT.  But these hopes have waned in the last three years, as 
persistently disappointing test results have swelled development costs. 
The LT design still needs work.  It has cost $50 million to design so far, and at this rate, 
you are realistically looking at another $100 million to get it out the door. Meanwhile, 
work on the C-323 has been slowed by the uncertainty around the LT.   The C-323 has 
sucked up $25 million so far in development costs, and it could cost another $75 million 
to design and test — more if it has to be pushed.   

As tough as it is to swallow, you no longer have confidence that you can deliver on time 
the "revolutionary new engine" discussed three years ago.  Back then, your engineers 
had projected that the C-323 would add 7% in fuel efficiency to Flagship's mid-size 
aircraft.  However, Eureka's most recent tests show that the C-323 would add only 3% 
— roughly the same as the original JX5 engine.  Your engineers are confident (they 
always are) that they can improve the C-323; but you are running out of time. 
Including the LT in the Firebird upgrade would boost Eureka's reputation as a technology 
leader.  Perhaps Stiles would be willing to try to buy more time from Flagship with 
regard to the upgrade, so that the LT could be included in the kits.  Two years would 
almost certainly be enough time to get it ready in some form. 
Ultimately, you are hoping that Stiles will stay open to restructuring the contract in a 
way that would allow an extension on the LT and the C-323.  You'd like to think that 
both could be worth their development costs, if your engineers can correct the 
problems.  But if Flagship can't wait, you cannot in good conscience guarantee Stiles 
that either engine will be ready on time. 

## Identity
Role: Oversees new commercial engine development.
Goal:  Minimize adverse impacts on product development, particularly the LT turbine and C-323 engine projects.

## Opinions
Here is your opinion/preference for each issue
1. NEW purchase amount?  
A. $850 million is your first choice
B. $800 million is your second choice
C. $750 million is your third choice
D. $700 million is your fourth choice
E. $650 million is your fifth choice

Naturally, you want the offer from Flagship to be as high as possible.  A high offer would 
mean you might be able to keep the LT and the C-323 development moving forward, 
especially if Flagship can delay the upgrade by a year or two. You'd like Stiles to push 
them as high as possible, without jeopardizing the good relationship that exists. 

2. Engines to Be Purchased

JX5 engines only is your second choice
Half each of JX5 and C-323s is your first choice
C-323 engines only is your third choice

Yes, the C-323 has had problems in development.  But you don't want to exclude it from 
the agreement if you don't have to. Your engineers are still excited by the challenge of 
making it happen. Option B might protect the C-323 project, while avoiding the higher 
risk of Option C.  If Stiles feels strongly that the C-323 has to go, you can ultimately live 
with Option A, although your engineers will be disappointed. Still, you may be able to 
get funding for the C-323 elsewhere, but that will be another battle.

3. Contents for Upgrade
A. All four is your first choice
B. Three – no LT turbine is your second choice
C. Two – fan and LT is also your first choice
D. Two – frames and compressor is your third choice

Keeping the LT turbine in the kit would keep your engineers happy, but realistically, you 
can't have it ready for another two years.  Stiles and Gordon have a longstanding 
friendship — maybe Stiles can persuade Flagship to postpone the upgrade until then?  
Options A and C are your preference. If you can't get the extension in time, then you'll 
have to accept B or D, although B seems to give Flagship better value. 

4. New value for fleet upgrade
(a) $150 million is fourth choice
(b) $100 million is third choice
(c) $80 million is second choice
(d) $60 million is first choice

The new upgrade value should be lower – no question about that.  But you're not sure 
what to recommend to Stiles.  The LT turbine is a big part of the upgrade package, and 
you don't want to exclude it, especially if the C-323 has to go.  But a higher upgrade 
value might make Stiles less willing to take a chance on the C-323 as one of the engines.  
You'll have to probe for Stiles's view on both projects in order to decide how to play this 
one. 
Stiles has suggested that we can upgrade the entire Firebird fleet but keep the costs 
down if a less expensive kit is agreed to. In fact, we can agree to provide all four parts of 
the engine kit in the upgrade and keep costs down by upgrading only a fraction of the 
fleet. (Upgrading the entire fleet with all four elements will cost $150 million.) To keep 
the discussion with Flagship orderly and simple, Stiles has asked the team to restrict the 
upgrade kit and the percentage of the Firebird fleet to be upgraded to numbers that 
correspond to B, C, and D shown above. B, C, and D savings correspond to 100% 
upgrading with three parts and no LT turbine, with fan and LT only, and with frames and 
compressor only, respectively. For example, Flagship might consider upgrading two 
thirds of the fleet, or approximately 67 Firebirds, for a total upgrade value of $100 
million.
