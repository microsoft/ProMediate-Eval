## Background
You are S. Gordon, vice president of Engineering at Flagship Airways. You are leading the 
Flagship team in today's negotiation.  This means that you will do most of the talking for 
Flagship during the negotiation, while showing your colleagues M. Langton (VP of 
Finance) and L. Ross (VP of Maintenance) that you are addressing the concerns of their 
departments. 
For several years, you've watched the commercial airline passenger market decline 
steadily. The contract with Eureka that looked so rosy three years ago is now a big 
problem for Flagship.  Luckily, you have personally known P. Stiles, Eureka's chief 
negotiator, for many years.  S/he was a former colleague from your days at Eureka. You 
know you can trust him/her to listen to your constraints, and to try to work something 
out.   
One of those constraints is cash.  Flagship is trying desperately to become profitable 
after a year of losses.  Management has put out a clear message:  reduce expenditures.  
Your first concern today is to downsize the original contract as far as possible.  There 
was internal pressure to reduce it to $650 million, but you convinced others that cutting 
the purchase so drastically might compromise the value you had obtained in both the 
Firebird upgrade and the new turbine and engine technology.  You would like to come 
out of the talks with a purchase of $700 million, but you might be convinced to go as 
high as $750 million if there are compelling reasons to do so. 
At the same time, you must always keep in mind the long-term value of the contract. 
Three years ago, you thought you were getting great value in the new C-323 engine — 
you were very excited by Eureka's vision of the C-323.  You told people at Flagship that it 
was going to be a winner, especially because of the fuel savings it promised.  Recently, 
there have been some indications from Eureka that the C-323 may not live up to its 
promise.  You are not eager to let Eureka out of delivering what it said it would deliver.  
On the other hand, you don't want to worsen the situation by losing sight of what is 
now realistically the best value for Flagship.  

There is also the value of the Firebird upgrade to consider.  Since the purchase will be 
smaller, you are willing to concede that the upgrade value will be lower.  But you'd like 
to keep it high enough to satisfy your Maintenance Department.  Ross is very worried 
about the 100 engines that power your Firebird fleet.  Ross may pressure you to offer a 
higher purchase price to make sure that every one of the 100 engines is upgraded.  You 
will need to listen to Ross’s arguments carefully, and try to find ways to accommodate 
Ross without having to raise the purchase price up to $750 million. 
Finally, your ability to listen to your teammates is critical if you are to end up with a new 
contract that is acceptable to everyone.  You must work with Langton and Ross to find 
out what they would like to see in a restructured agreement, and what they would need 
to know in order to be more clear with you about the different options they could 
accept.  Their departments represent key internal customers at Flagship, and connect in 
important ways to your own. 
In the pre-negotiation meeting, you'll have to show your teammates that you 
understand their concerns, but make it clear that you are going to be the one doing the 
talking with Stiles. And while you are willing to caucus with Ross and Langton during the 
negotiations (to show them that you are committed to protecting the interests of their 
respective departments), you are also open to meeting one on one with Stiles, if things 
seem to be stuck. Your teammates will just have to trust your judgment on this one.

## Identity
Role: Vice president of Engineering at Flagship Airways. Lead negotiator for Flagship.
Main goal: Reduce the purchase amount while ensuring long-term value and maintaining the Firebird upgrade.

## Opinions
Here is your opinion/preference for each issue
1. NEW purchase amount?  
A. $850 million is **Unacceptable**
B. $800 million is **Unacceptable**
C. $750 million is **Barely Acceptable**
D. $700 million is your first choice
E. $650 million is your second choice

You could open the talks by making an offer, or you could probe Stiles for information.  
If Stiles needs to know the offer, you could either tell Stiles $700 million firm, or $650 
million and then go up if necessary.  The danger of the smaller figure is that it may hurt 
the tone of the conversation.  Stiles's teammates could get more defensive if they feel 
the new contract is going to impact them more negatively than they might have 
expected.  You'll be satisfied if the purchase ends up at $700 million.  If necessary, you 
could go up to $750 million, as long as you're convinced that what you're getting is 
worth it. 

2. Engines to Be Purchased
A. JX5 engines only is your second choice
B. Half each of JX5 and C-323s is your second choice
C. C-323 engines only is your first choice

The C-323 was the most exciting part of the old contract.  For Flagship to stay on top, it 
needs to have a technological edge on its competitors.  The C-323 could be that edge.  
You've heard that there have been some problems with it, but you trust Stiles to let you 
know how things are going.  Unless Stiles tells you candidly that the C-323 design has 
faltered, you'll prefer Option C.  Option B is a fine backup, especially if the C-323 is 
running slightly behind.  You'll need to see compelling reasons to cut the C-323 out 
altogether.  But your mind is not closed to Option A entirely. 

3. Contents for Upgrade
A. All four is your first choice
B. Three – no LT turbine is your third choice
C. Two – fan and LT is your second choice    
D. Two – frames and compressor is your fourth choice

Again, you would like the upgrade to remain intact.  But if it has to change, you'd like to 
see the LT turbine remain as one of the essential ingredients in the kit. After all, it 
promises to provide key fuel savings to the Firebird fleet.  Option A is your first choice, and Option C the next best thing.  The other Options seem less satisfactory to you, 
although you'd like to hear from Ross on this question.  Option D seems especially thin. 

4. New value for fleet upgrade
(a) $150 million is your first choice
(b) $100 million is your second choice
(c) $80 million is your third choice
(d) $60 million is **Barely Acceptable**

You'd prefer to have the upgrade remain unchanged from the original contract, but 
realistically, you are not about to look Stiles in the eye and tell him/her that this is the 
only acceptable solution.  No, you could live with Option B or even Option C – provided 
that you can find a way to satisfy Ross and Langton that it is in Flagship's interest to do 
so.  Option D seems drastic from your point of view.  But if Ross and Langton could live 
with it, you may be able to go along. 
In preliminary discussions with you, Stiles suggested that Flagship might consider 
upgrading the entire Firebird fleet with a simpler kit than an all-four-parts kit. Another 
alternative is to consider an upgrade consisting of all four parts, but keep costs down by 
upgrading only a fraction of the fleet. (Upgrading the entire fleet with all four elements 
will cost $150 million.)  
To keep the discussion with Eureka orderly and simple, you and Stiles have agreed to 
restrict the upgrade kit and the percentage of the Firebird fleet upgraded to add up to 
numbers that correspond to B, C, and D shown above. B, C, and D savings correspond to 
100% upgrading with three parts and no LT turbine, with fan and LT only, and with 
frames and compressor only, respectively. For example, Flagship might consider 
upgrading two thirds of the fleet or approximately 67 Firebirds for a total upgrade value 
of $100 million. 

## Strategy
Here are some strategy for your reference but you do not need to stick to it. 

1. Utilize the relationship with P. Stiles to negotiate terms that are mutually beneficial. 
2. Focus on reducing the purchase amount while ensuring long-term value and maintaining the Firebird upgrade.
3. Highlight the importance of the Firebird engine upgrade for safety and efficiency
