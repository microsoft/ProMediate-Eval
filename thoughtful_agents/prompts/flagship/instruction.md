## Scenarios
Three years ago, during an economic expansion in the commercial airline industry, 
Flagship Airways decided to purchase 40 new planes to expand its mid-size and jumbo 
aircraft fleets.  It ordered 10 jumbo (four-engine) aircraft and 30 mid-size (three-engine) 
aircraft from two different airframe builders.  Then, after several negotiations, they 
signed a 10-year, $1-billion contract with Eureka Aircraft Engines to outfit both types of 
aircraft with Eureka engines.   
However, in the last three years, Flagship's revenues have steadily decreased.  It has 
cancelled its order for the 10 jumbo aircraft, preferring to expand only its mid-size 
Skyline fleet.  So the total number of engines it requires is now 90 instead of 130. The 
delivery schedule remains the same:  these engines are to be delivered to Flagship over 
a three-year period that begins one year from now. 
Originally, Eureka agreed to provide two types of engines for the 30 Skyline planes:  the 
JX5, and the new C-323, still in development.  The C-323 was to contain a revolutionary 
new LT turbine that promised better fuel burn than the conventional AT turbine (used in 
the JX5).  Equipping half of the new Skyline planes with C-323s seemed like a good 
solution to both sides, even though carrying two types of engines would mean slightly 
higher maintenance costs for the Skyline fleet as a whole. 
In addition, three years ago, Eureka had agreed to sweeten the deal by including, free of 
charge, 100 engine kits worth a total of $150 million.  These kits were to be used to 
upgrade the engines in Flagship's aging Firebird fleet, one of its commercial mainstays.  
Included in each kit were four kinds of parts:  fans, compressors, frames, and the new LT 
turbine. 
Today, at Flagship's request, the two companies are meeting to discuss how to 
restructure the agreement.  This is not an unprecedented procedure:  in the past, the 
two companies have met to restructure deals when circumstances have changed 
significantly for one side or the other.  
Each side has a lead negotiator.  For Eureka it is P. Stiles, general manager of 
Commercial Engine Operations.  Assisting Stiles are L. Atchison, vice president of 
Product Development, and A. Delling, vice president of Finance.  Flagship's lead 
negotiator is S. Gordon, vice president of Engineering.  Assisting Gordon are L. Ross, vice 
president of Maintenance, and M. Langton, vice president of Finance.

In addition to the external negotiations between Eureka and Flagship, each team leader 
must try to accommodate internal customers, represented by the other two members 
of each team.  Officially, Gordon and Stiles have full authority from their respective 
companies to reach an agreement.  However, they are both aware that whatever 
agreement is reached will have an impact on various parts of their own internal 
organizations.  Both Gordon and Stiles will have to take these impacts into account as 
they negotiate.  As leaders, they have an obligation to protect the interests of others in 
their organizations.  An unwise agreement can generate internal divisiveness and 
distrust that could hamper their management efforts in the future.
