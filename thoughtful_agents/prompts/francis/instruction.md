## Scenario
St. Francis Hospital, a 1,200-bed nonprofit medical complex located in a large midwestern urban center, is facing significant financial and organizational challenges. The hospital's Board and executive leadership have been struggling to manage tighter federal regulations, prospective payment and managed care systems, and inter-professional tensions.

Several years ago, the Board hired a new chief financial officer (CFO), C. Marshall, who believed that managing physician practice was essential to controlling hospital costs and ensuring long-term financial viability. Marshall, along with the CEO G. Bennett, supported the development of a new Medical Management Model (MMM) under the leadership of Dr. M. Mason, the chief of medicine.

The MMM is designed to make physicians responsible and accountable for the operation of all medical services, supported by a new Management Information System (MIS). The model aims to improve patient care, manage costs, and resolve inter-professional differences. The CEO and CFO have backed the model, but other members of the hospital community, such as the vice president for nursing N. MacNamara and senior attending physician Dr. A. Parker, have expressed opposition or demanded compensating benefits.

The hospital initially tested the MMM in three units within the Department of Medicine, including the Cardiology Unit. The demonstration phase met its objectives, but expanding the model hospital-wide would require significant organizational restructuring and additional budgetary resources.

The CEO has convened a meeting with five major players to reach an agreement on these issues. If they fail to agree, the Board of Trustees will make the decisions, which could lead to undesirable outcomes for the managers involved.

The negotiation involves balancing the interests of various stakeholders:

G. Bennett (CEO): Focused on the long-term financial viability and competitive position of the hospital. Supports the MMM but is concerned about losing key staff like Parker and maintaining nursing quality.
N. MacNamara (Senior Vice President for Nursing): Advocates for a collaborative professional partnership between physicians and nurses. Concerned about the impact of the MMM on nursing staff and patient care.
M. Mason (Chief of Medicine): A strong proponent of the MMM, aiming to expand it hospital-wide and enhance physician leadership. Sees the MMM as a way to improve hospital operations and advance personal career goals.
A. Parker (Senior Attending Physician): Opposes the MMM, fearing it will lead to administrative control over medical decisions and restrict physicians' autonomy. Prefers maintaining the current system or finding a compromise that increases nursing input.
C. Marshall (CFO): Strongly supports the MMM for its financial benefits and improved management practices. Believes in the importance of financial accountability and efficient resource utilization.
