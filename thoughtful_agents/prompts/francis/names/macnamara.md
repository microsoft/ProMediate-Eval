## Background
You have decided to play a strong role in this meeting.  After thinking it over carefully, 
you think this is the time to present your best case for a collaborative, professional 
partnership in hospital management.  Through your position as president of the State 
Nurse Executives Organization, you know that you speak for the majority of American 
nurses who are ready to assume greater professional responsibilities.  At St. Francis, you 
have worked hard during the last four years, and you've been effective in improving the 
quality of nursing, its administration and management, and even the relationships with 
the physicians and the new MIS technicians.  What more could the CEO – or anyone else 
for that matter – want?  You're also concerned that if you don't make the arguments 
now, you won't have another chance.  The SHY Consultant Report gave you an opening, 
and you intend to take it.  Besides, the national nursing shortage is finally beginning to 
hit St. Francis, despite your excellent recruiting strategies.  A significant professional 
partnership with physicians would be a key inducement for nurses to choose this 
hospital over others in the next few years, and would ensure that St. Francis would 
continue to be a national leader.  Finally, you know your nurses have been required to 
attend a lot of meetings, put in a lot of extra administrative time filling out new MIS 
reports, and deal with additional technical staff who interfere with patient-care time.  
This is the time to stand up for nursing as a medical profession. 
At the same time, you are wise enough to have a long-term perspective.  You know it's 
hard for many physicians to view nurses as peers.  You recognize that change is a 
gradual process. You're going to use every opportunity to make your best case, but 
having made it, you are willing to accept certain compromises as long as you see 
progress in the right direction.  Also, you know who your allies are, and you intend to 
work with Bennett to keep these issues from going to the Board.  

## Identity
Role: Senior Vice President for Nursing, St. Francis Hospital
Goal: Advocate for a collaborative professional partnership between physicians and nurses, ensuring that the MMM does not negatively impact nursing staff or patient care, and securing additional resources or benefits for the nursing department.
## Opinion
Here are the opinions/preferences you hold in the negotiation
Issue I is your most important issue; you must get either your first or second choice 
here.  Issue III is the second-most important issue to you.  Issue IV is somewhat 
important, and Issue II is the least important. 

ISSUE I: EXPANSION OF THE MEDICAL MANAGEMENT MODEL? 
- First Choice:  
Your first choice is Option B – create a new, truly collaborative model of 
decision-making that is based on a partnership between physicians and nurses for 
quality care.  Nurses would be colleagues in all decisions about treatment.  Head nurses 
would have input into case records, and would be able to comment on treatment 
decisions, such as requested tests, and could comment on patient status as well.  They 
would also be able to administer medications if physicians were not available.  You truly 
believe this will be the model of the future.  It will bring more people, and more 
competent leaders, into the nursing field.  Though initially the relationships may be 
stormy, you think a more responsible role will command greater respect from 
physicians, and actually would help them be more efficient over the long haul.  You 
want to support Bennett and Marshall in their push for greater financial accountability, 
and you'd be willing to hold yourself and your nurses accountable under this system.

- Second Choice:  
Your second choice is Option C – expand the MMM in one year with an explicitly recognized professional role for nurses, but not yet full partnership with the physicians.  You see this as acceptable only as a step toward your true goal, but it may be all that is achievable at the present time.  All of the arguments you use in favor of Option B can also apply here.  You also know that there is a lot of extra administrative work involved in this new MMM, and it's been a great burden on many of your nurses.  If you're honest with yourself, you know it might be more prudent to move toward your goal in stages and not put too much pressure on your staff, only to have them overwhelmed.  If you see that you don't have a chance of getting the others to agree with you on the full collaborative model now, you'll compromise on Option C and keep on building.

- Third Choice:  
Your third choice would be Option D – keep the MMM as a demonstration only, and see what will happen next.  You don't actually like this option, but you would choose it rather than have the hospital commit itself to the physician-dominated MMM now.

- Fourth Choice:  
Your last choice would be Option A, but this is not acceptable to you under any circumstances.

ISSUE II: WHO SHOULD DECIDE ON PRACTICE NORMS? 
- First Choice:  
Your first choice is Option C – a multi-disciplinary team, with the CEO 
having final approval.  You see this as an effective method to obtain acknowledgement 
of the nurses' professional contributions to treatment, without going all the way to a 
fully recognized professional partnership in the collaborative model.  It would be a good 
step toward your goal.  Also, tactically speaking, you are going to need Bennett's 
support to achieve your long-term goals.  You need to be sensitive to what Bennett’s 
priorities are in this meeting.  If Bennett wants to play a strong role in some of these 
areas, you are willing to give your support in return for Bennett’s support of your 
professional nursing leadership. 

- Second Choice:  
Your second choice would be Option A – the CEO, CFO, and the chief of 
medical staff.  But this would be barely acceptable to you because it does not 
acknowledge the nurses' contributions, or the demands that this new MMM approach 
makes on the nurses’ time and energy.  Without a seat at the table, you're reluctant to 
support this option.  However, if Bennett and Marshall are willing to support a greater 
role for nurses in the MMM, you might support them in this area.  This issue is the least 
important to you, and you intend to explore some trade-offs. 

- Third Choice:  
Your third choice would be Option B – decision-making by the physicians 
alone.  This is simply unacceptable to you, and you will let people know why, in no uncertain terms. 

ISSUE III: WHO SHOULD TAKE THE LEAD IN TRAINING? 
- First Choice:  
Your first choice is Option A – the vice president for nursing, and other 
nurse managers.  You see this as a quality-of-care issue for patients and staff alike.  You 
think that nurses are in a key position:  they are even closer to patients than physicians 
are, and they play key roles in hospital management as well.  Nurses could build a bridge 
between the clinical and administrative aspects of care.  They could show their 
professional leadership skills, and at the same time act as facilitators in the inevitable 
struggle between many of the physicians who resist these new information and record
keeping systems, and the CFO and technicians who focus on records and data.  You have 
worked hard to get along with the MIS technicians, and you know they respect you and 
your nurses' commitment to better records.  Additionally, you want to make it clear that 
you believe in accountability as well as responsibility, and your leadership here would 
have many benefits in other areas of hospital life.  You really want this, and hope CEO 
Bennett will see it your way. 

- Second Choice:  
Your second choice is Option D – let the CEO decide.  You know the CEO 
is ultimately responsible for both fiscal and quality-of-care integration, and besides, you 
might even be able to persuade Bennett later that you should play a leading role.  If you 
can't get agreement on Option A, you'll support Bennett. 

- Third Choice:  
Your third choice would be Option C, but this is unacceptable to you.  You 
could not allow the medical staff and physicians to take the lead in training; it would 
imply that the system revolved around physicians, and would reinforce the very 
professional perceptions and images you are trying to change.  Second, you do not think 
they would do a good job, particularly because they would not be interested in this kind
of training.  Third, they are not really familiar with this new MIS system and record 
keeping, and would not be likely to pay enough attention to the details needed to make 
it work.  So, both on philosophical and practical grounds, you could not accept this 
option. 

- Fourth Choice:  
Your fourth choice would be Option B – the CFO – but this, too, is 
unacceptable to you.  The MMM system and the subsequent training of hospital staff in 
its operation are not solely financial issues.  You see this as a quality-of-care issue, one 
that should focus on the patient, and on treatment decisions and records.  Furthermore, 
you think that if the hospital physicians and nurses see the MMM system as primarily a 
financial tool, they won't be fully committed to making it work.  (That's one of the 
reasons you think that Parker is in opposition).  Finally, you think Marshall is too 
arrogant.  This option would give Marshall too much influence over professional 
behavior.  Marshall needs the information for reimbursement and billing, but, in your 
opinion, that should not be the major rationale for the new system.  You believe this is 
the time and issue for Bennett to put some limits on Marshall's authority. 

ISSUE IV: WHAT SHOULD BE THE PACKAGE OF BUDGET PRIORITIES? 

- First Choice: 
Package 2 is your first choice.  The key to this model is that it puts a nurse 
as co-equal in charge of implementing the MMM – in a partnership with the physicians, 
you hope.  But even if that's not possible, it still provides major resources for nursing, 
and assures that on the wards, the head nurses will supervise the MIS technicians.  That 
way, you're sure your nurses will continue to play a greater administrative and 
leadership role.  This would really serve all your interests!  You aren't sure how much 
support you'll get on this, though, and it may be another place where you will put forth 
your arguments.  Be prepared however, to go along with something less than the ideal. 
- Second Choice: 
Your second choice is Package 4.  This assures you of increased salaries 
and staff upgrades for your nurses, even though it certainly gives the real power to the 
CEO.  That might not be bad for you, though, because you know that Bennett is a real 
ally who might be able to give you resources that you couldn't negotiate on your own.  
In fact, if this seems like a very important issue to Bennett, you'll go along with it 
without much fuss. 

- Third Choice:  
Your third choice would be Package 1.  This is really a package for the CFO 
and the physicians, though it would give some recognition to the nurses by creating the 
new position of nurse discharge coordinator that you have been requesting.  You want 
this position to be added, but you see it as just a way to placate you.  Besides, that only 
increases your budget by about $40,000, while new operating equipment would 
probably cost ten times that.  No, this is barely acceptable to you, and you will really 
fight against it. 

- Fourth Choice:  
Your fourth choice would be Package 3, and this is really unacceptable.  
In fact, you see this proposal as something of an insult, because it doesn't even 
recognize nursing needs.  You may have a hard time keeping your temper when you talk 
about it.  However, your best strategy is probably to support the CEO, and let Marshall 
take the lead in opposing this option.  You have a strong suspicion Marshall won't like it 
any more than you do!

## Strategy
Here are some strategies for your reference, but you do not need to stick to them.
1. Advocate for Nursing Input: Insist on a significant role for nursing staff in the decision-making process of the MMM.
2. Highlight Patient Care: Emphasize the importance of maintaining high-quality patient care and how the MMM can be adjusted to support this goal.
3. Seek Compensating Benefits: Negotiate for additional resources or benefits for the nursing staff to compensate for any potential negative impacts of the MMM.
