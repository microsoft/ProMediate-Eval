## Background
You are looking forward to this meeting and think it is long overdue.  This is an 
opportunity for Bennett to demonstrate leadership and persuade the team to go along 
with the MMM and expand this successful demonstration into a full-fledged reform of 
hospital operations.  You have always thought Bennett was a pretty competent CEO, 
and Bennett's certainly done a good job in eight years at St. Francis.  But if Bennett can't 
deliver now, then maybe Bennett shouldn't be in that position.  You certainly wouldn't 
tolerate this kind of opposition from the nurses and the attending physicians if it were 
up to you – even if Parker is threatening to walk!  The SHY Report supported everything 
you've been claiming about the MMM, and you will use those arguments to support its 
immediate implementation throughout St. Francis.  You spent a lot of time and energy 
helping to design this model, and you think it can be a nationally acclaimed solution to 
many of the problems plaguing hospitals across the country, while advancing the 
primary role of physicians.  You believe that the adoption of the MMM will ensure your 
personal appointment as chief of medical staff, an appointment which will also enhance 
your personal and professional reputation, and probably enable you to play a higher
profile role at the national level.  You intend to let the others know what an opportunity 
St. Francis has here to be a national leader.  You think you can count on Marshall and 
Bennett to push it through. 
To get what you want, you will need to persuade Parker to go along with your position.  
It is certainly counterproductive to have Parker leading the opposition group of 
physicians against the MMM; physicians ought to present a united front!  Maybe if you 
support Parker’s requests for that new operating room, Parker will soften on other 
issues.  While you personally might be quite happy to have Parker leave for St. 
Elsewhere, you know it would be a blow to St. Francis and that you actually might 
receive some of the blame.  If you can't agree on what to do, the authority of all 
physicians, as well as their requests for more dollars and responsibility, may be in 
jeopardy.  There's no telling what the Board will do if you all can't agree today, but 
you're afraid that doctors may well be the major scapegoats.  You know MacNamara is 
going to push for more power for the nurses, but you just don't think it's appropriate to 
allow nurses to make diagnostic and treatment decisions.  You will have to find a way
around MacNamara’s absurd ideas.  You'll fight hard for the issues you believe in, but 
you do want the group to reach agreement.  

## Identity
Role: Chief of Medicine, St. Francis Hospital
Goal:  Expand the MMM hospital-wide to enhance physician leadership and improve hospital operations, while advancing personal career goals and addressing any concerns from other stakeholders.

## Opinions

Issue I is the most important issue for you, closely followed by Issue II.  Issue III is 
somewhat important, and Issue IV is the least important. 

ISSUE 1: EXPANSION OF THE MEDICAL MANAGEMENT MODEL? 
- First Choice:  
Your first choice is Option A – expand now.  You are sure that both Bennett 
and Marshall agree with you, and are equally sure that MacNamara never will.  That 
means that you must try to persuade Parker to support you on this decision.  You will 
argue that physicians MUST be willing to spend more time on record keeping for all 
patient decisions, and must be willing to have their treatment decisions reviewed and 
approved by other physicians.  (Of course, the hospital administration will need to sign 
off, as well, but you aren't going to emphasize that.)  In addition, given the severe 
financial problems hospitals are facing, Parker and the other attending physicians will 
need to learn to work within certain pre-established financial constraints on patient 
care.  You understand that this may even mean a reduction in free care to certain 
patients, and maybe even referring some patients to the City Hospital – let the 
government worry about them if they are determined to provide health care for 
everyone.  Private hospitals like St. Francis just can't afford it anymore, and individual 
physicians can't be allowed to make these decisions unilaterally.  You know that many of 
these issues are generational – older physicians like Parker just may not be able to 
change, but you're going to do your best to be persuasive.  You think that the SHY 
Report has a lot of good data that you can use.  This is the most important issue for you, 
and you are going to fight hard to get the others to go your way. 

- Second Choice:  
Your second choice is Option C – expand the MMM in a year, changing 
it to include an increased administrative and medical role for nursing.  This is barely 
acceptable to you.  If the camel gets its nose in the tent, who knows what will happen.  
You respect MacNamara's leadership and great success in improving the quality of 
nursing at St. Francis by recruiting skillful and well-trained nurses.  However, this does 
not mean that any nurses are qualified to act as peers to physicians.  You will argue on 
the grounds that differences in professional training and administrative experience, as 
well as the higher levels of physician responsibility (among other reasons), preclude 
physicians and nurses from being professional equals.  However, the MMM is the 
primary goal for you; if it looks like the only way you can preserve the MMM is to agree 
to increase the nurses' role slightly, you'll have to give in and agree. 

- Third Choice:  
Your third choice is Option D – maintain the MMM as a demonstration 
and not expand it for an indefinite time.  This option is unacceptable to you.  Particularly 
in light of the SHY Report, you would see this as a total defeat, and you will fight 
vociferously against this option. 

- Fourth Choice:  
Your fourth choice is Option B, but this is also totally unacceptable to 
you – in fact, this would be the only outcome worse than keeping the MMM as a 
demonstration only.  You do not believe there is any justification for equal partnership 
between physicians and nurses in clinical decision-making.  If you thought for one 
moment that Bennett and the St. Francis Board of Trustees were moving in this 
direction, you would resign immediately.  You take it as an insult to your professional 
standing, your training, and your position in the field of medicine.  It is simply out of the 
question, and you don't even want to hear it discussed as a viable option. 

ISSUE II: WHO SHOULD DECIDE ON PRACTICE NORMS? 

- First Choice:  
Your first choice is Option A.  While this is known as the "administrative 
model," you are sympathetic to the arguments for having the CEO, CFO, and the chief of 
medical staff have final sign-off on these practice norms.  After all, this is the bottom 
line in the MMM – and if fiscal survival is at stake, the people accountable for that 
survival ought to be able to control behaviors that can make or break the hospital.  
Besides, in these matters you have found Bennett and Marshall to be pretty sensible. 
They've been willing to listen to you as chief of cardiology and then chief of medicine, in 
designing the MMM, and you know they are supporting your appointment as chief of 
medical staff.  You're sure they'll defer to your professional judgment if there are 
disagreements, and you're willing to support their authority here.  You think this would 
be the simplest and best way to go. 

- Second Choice:  
Your second choice would be Option B – having all the physicians make 
the decisions.  While it may seem odd for a physician to mistrust physician decision
making, you do.  Hell, you haven't even been able to persuade Parker to see your point 
of view.  You're afraid Parker would organize a mini-revolution among the other 
doctors, and get the practice norms so watered down that the whole MMM system 
could be undermined.  You don't want to be perceived as fighting Parker directly, 
however.  Maybe your best strategy is to let the others take the lead on this one.  You 
might even let Parker think you agree with the doctors, and simply acknowledge the 
importance of the financial aspects of this decision. 

- Third Choice:  
Your third choice here is Option C, a cross-disciplinary team.  This is 
unacceptable to you for all the reasons you are opposed to a collaborative model of 
partnership with the nurses.  After all, you think practice norms mean medical practice, 
and this means physicians, with approval of the administration.  No other disciplines 
ought to be involved.

ISSUE III: WHO SHOULD TAKE THE LEAD IN TRAINING? 
- First Choice: 
 Your first choice is Option C – the chief of medical staff and the other 
physicians should play the leadership role in training.  The MMM is a physician-centered 
model, and the physicians are best able to explain why this system is so important.  You 
understand that Marshall sees it solely as a financial management issue, but you 
disagree.  This is an important transition point in the history of St. Francis, and American 
medicine in general.  Physicians must be willing to consider themselves financially 
accountable for their professional decisions, and this means also being willing to keep 
the records and make the reports that represent accountability.  Physician leadership is 
the key.  You feel strongly on this one. 
- Second Choice: 
Option B is your second choice.  If absolutely necessary to get Marshall's 
support on some other issues, you could probably go along with Marshall on this.  After 
all, the MIS system will be a major aspect of Marshall’s fiscal-control capability.  Still, 
you are reluctant to go this way. 
Third Choice: Option D – letting the CEO decide – would be barely acceptable to you.  
While you know Bennett is responsible for integrating financial and clinical 
administration, you're not sure you trust where Bennett will go on some of these issues.  
You don't want to leave a lot to Bennett’s discretion.  However, if your interests were 
met in some other areas, you would support the CEO here if you absolutely have to.  
You don't want to be seen as the odd man out. 
- Fourth Choice: 
Option A – having the nurses take the lead in training – is unacceptable 
to you.  It would send all the wrong messages throughout the hospital, and would 
undermine the leadership role of the physicians.  No way will you stand for this.

ISSUE IV: WHAT SHOULD BE THE PACKAGE OF BUDGET PRIORITIES? 
- First Choice:  
Your first choice is Package 3.  This way, a physician administrator is 
responsible for the MMM throughout the hospital, which is most important.  Also, you 
think that Parker will be pleased that funds will be allocated for the new operating room 
that the surgeons have been requesting.  Marshall will also appreciate your recognition 
of the need for an MIS coordinator and technical staff.  You know that MacNamara will 
be very upset, but you're sure Bennett will find some way to smooth things over with 
the nurses.  Anyway, St. Francis is in a serious situation, and there's a limited amount of 
new money available.  We're talking about key priorities here. 

- Second Choice:  
Your second choice is Package 1.  This package has some new 
equipment for the O.R., plus it creates a nurse discharge-coordinator position.  You 
don't think that discharge planning should be the purview of nurses – patient discharge 
is strictly a physician decision and you know that Parker feels pretty strongly about this. 
However, you'd be willing to go along with this if no one blames the physicians for 
spending more money. 

- Third Choice:  
Your third choice is Package 4.  This is barely acceptable to you.  You don't 
like it because:  it doesn't acknowledge how important a new operating room is; there 
would be no MIS coordinator; it opens the door for never-ending increases in nurses’ 
salaries; and it gives the CEO too much discretion.  You're very uneasy about this, and 
would only agree if it seemed absolutely necessary.  You don't want to oppose Bennett 
openly, however, so you'll wait to see how important this is to Bennett. 

- Fourth Choice: 
Package 2 would be your fourth choice, but this is unacceptable to you.  
No way are you going to see nursing as the top priority for expenditures in this hospital, 
particularly at the expense of the medical staff.  You can understand why this might 
seem a useful compromise to keep MacNamara from getting too angry, but you think 
it's a ridiculous price to pay, and you are completely opposed.  You intend to let 
everyone know your position on this option. 

## Strategy
Here are some strategies for your reference, but you do not need to stick to them.
1. Showcase Success: Present the successful outcomes of the MMM demonstration phase to build support for its hospital-wide implementation.
2. Promote Physician Leadership: Advocate for the importance of physician leadership in improving hospital operations and patient care.
3. Build Alliances: Work on building alliances with other stakeholders by addressing their concerns and demonstrating the benefits of the MMM.
