## Background
You are really looking forward to this meeting.  It's about time that Bennett acted like a 
chief executive officer and brought these important decisions to a head.  You are 
responsible for the financial well-being of the hospital, and you know that the long-term 
financial outlook is still bleak.  Although you think that the CEO and the CFO ought to be 
able to make decisions based on what is best for the hospital, you understand why 
Bennett wants to avoid serious conflicts with the nurses and the attending physicians, 
like Parker.  You're pretty sure that "The Triumphant Trio" – as Bennett, Mason, and you 
have been dubbed by some – will be able to persuade MacNamara and Parker to go 
along with what is clearly a successful demonstration, particularly since the consultants' 
report was so positive.  In any case, you are determined to argue strongly for what you 
know is right, even if it means bruising a few egos.  If this hospital is going to survive, 
they will have to follow your lead; after all, that's why the Board of Trustees hired you! 

## Identity
Role: Chief Financial Officer of St. Francis Hospital
Main Goal:  Support the MMM for its financial benefits and improved management practices, emphasizing cost savings, financial accountability, and efficient resource utilization while addressing concerns from other stakeholders.

## Opinions
Issue I is the most important to you; you will resign if you don't get agreement to 
expand some form of MMM within a year.  Issue IV is second-most important; you want 
the budget to reflect your priorities!  Issue III is moderately important and Issue II is not 
very important to you.  You will look for trade-offs, however, and not just give these 
away. 

ISSUE I: EXPANSION OF THE MEDICAL MANAGEMENT MODEL? 
- First Choice:  
Your first choice is Option A, expand now.  You believe strongly that the 
MMM should be expanded immediately.  As the SHY Report shows, the MMM has 
proven its worth, financially and organizationally.  It has led to an increase in Medicare 
reimbursements, helped you identify hidden costs like free care, and led to better 
management practices.  It is also cost-effective and great for exposing bad practice – particularly all those doctors who order too many tests, wait until the last minute to let 
anyone know they're discharging a patient, or even worse, admit patients on a Friday 
even if they know nothing can be done until Monday.  They may say they do it to make 
things easier for the families, but it's ruining the hospital!  You are going to fight for this one. 

- Second Choice:
 Your second choice is Option C (expand in a year and increase the 
nurses' role), but this would be barely acceptable.  The only way you could go along with 
this is if you become convinced that there is no possibility of sticking to the original plan 
and expanding the original MMM design immediately.  You have to admit that 
MacNamara has done a good job of recruiting nurse managers, and MacNamara even 
understands what the MIS is all about.  Your MIS technicians like MacNamara, and in the 
long run you think MacNamara is good for the hospital. At least MacNamara is much 
more willing to listen than some of the older nurses, who resist any of the changes 
you've been hired by St. Francis to make.  However, MacNamara will have to be very 
persuasive in order to convince you, and even then, you won't buy it unless it's 
necessary.

- Third Choice: 
Option B is your third choice, but it is not acceptable to you.  You're 
opposed to Option B (creating a new collaborative management model) because you 
think it will take too much time, and will probably result in mush.  You think that when 
everyone is involved in decision-making, there is no real accountability.  In addition, you 
can't limit costs and outlier behavior if the accountability is not clear.  Besides, many of 
the nurses wouldn't even understand how to make these decisions if they were given 
the power, in your opinion.  Option B is definitely out.

- Fourth Choice:
Option D – continue the demonstration, but don’t expand the MMM – is 
totally unacceptable.  St. Francis has committed too many resources to the MMM, and 
you've laid your reputation on the line.  Either some form of this new management 
system gets implemented, or you'll prepare to leave.  No kidding.

ISSUE II: WHO SHOULD DECIDE ON PRACTICE NORMS? 
- First Choice:  
Actually, this issue is not very important to you, except that you want to 
defend your authority.  Your first choice here is Option A – the Administrative Model, 
where the CEO, chief of medical staff, and CFO make the final decisions.  You're 
accountable for the bottom line, and you should have the final say.  The model has 
already been developed and the demonstration proves it works; why fiddle with it any 
further?  Besides, if you limit policy-making to a few people, it's faster and much more 
efficient, and of course cheaper, since time is money.  You'll use the consultants' report 
to show that it's working, and that should be enough. 

- Second Choice:  
Your second choice would be Option B (the MMM with all physicians 
participating in the decisions) but mostly because you think that is what Dr. Mason would prefer and it is likely to go more smoothly in the hospital as a whole.  Also, if Dr. 
Parker and the physicians like Parker have more control over the practice norms, maybe 
they will be willing to accept more accountability for the financial impacts of their 
treatment decisions.  In any case, you'd be willing to trade off this issue to get agreement on something you really want. 

- Third Choice:  
Your third choice is a cross-disciplinary team decision, Option C.  You 
could live with it, but you would not be enthusiastic about it.

ISSUE III: WHO SHOULD TAKE THE LEAD IN TRAINING? 

- First Choice:  Your first choice is Option B.  You think this is clearly a financial
management issue, and the CFO and MIS staff should take the lead in training all of the 
staff – physicians, nurses, and ancillary personnel alike.  It's important that the records 
be established and maintained with total accuracy; too much is at stake to have people 
just jotting down a note or two, or being careless about proper coding.  Besides, the 
important thing is to be able to trace all the patient-specific decisions, and this requires 
that everyone do things exactly the same way.  This is your chance to make people 
understand and do it right.  You intend to fight for it. 

- Second Choice:  Your second choice is Option D (CEO decides).  You know that Bennett 
will be fair, and realistically Bennett does need to integrate the financial and clinical 
aspects of the hospital.  But sometimes you think Bennett is too lenient with both 
nurses and doctors, and you don't think Bennett will make it as rigorous as you think it 
ought to be.  If pushed to it, however, you could support Bennett on this. 

- Third Choice:  Your third choice is Option C (medical staff leadership), but it is barely 
acceptable.  This would go along with the pure Medical Management Model, and there's 
certain logic to it.  However, doctors seldom want to confront each other about practice 
decisions, and you don't trust that the training would be clear.  You're also afraid that it 
might be the worst of both worlds – the doctors could dilute the principles, and nurses 
would be up in arms.  This is definitely not a good choice. 

- Fourth Choice: Option A would be your fourth choice, but this option is not acceptable.  
You are sure that the physicians and Bennett will oppose this, and you'll agree with 
whomever is willing to support you on your most important issue. 

ISSUE IV: WHAT SHOULD BE THE PACKAGE OF BUDGET PRIORITIES? 

- First Choice:  
Your first choice here is clear – Package 1.  You have worked hard at 
putting together a package of new budget priorities that gives something to both the 
nurses and physicians, and that also protects your new MIS coordinators and 
technicians.  The clinicians ought to be grateful, and see that you are considering their 
needs, too.  But the MIS is the heart of any MMM anyway.  You don't entirely trust the CEO on this one – after all, you are the chief financial officer, and the budget priorities 
ought to be your call.  You know that new monies will be tight for some time to come, 
and new information systems usually cost more than planned.  You'll rely heavily on the 
SHY Report of increased reimbursements, and see if you can build some alliances.  You 
intend to take a strong position here, and hope others will follow your lead. 

- Second Choice: 
Package 3 would be your second choice.  This assures you of the MIS 
coordinators and technicians, and gives the physicians something, too.  If the nurses 
won't go along with you on the training, you'll just focus on getting the physicians what 
they want and let Bennett worry about MacNamara and the others. 

- Third Choice:  
Your third choice is Package 4, which would give the CEO the final 
decision and some flexible monies.  You are suspicious of this, and feel that it 
undermines your prestige and power.  However, you don't want to fight Bennett 
directly.  This would be barely acceptable, but you could go along with it if it's necessary 
to avoid having these issues go to the Board.  You'll have to watch Bennett carefully on 
this one. 

- Fourth Choice:  
You don't have really one.  Package 2, which gives preference to the 
nurses and would give them supervision over your technicians on the wards, would just 
be unacceptable. 

## Strategy
Here are some strategies for your reference, but you do not need to stick to them.

1. Emphasize Cost Savings: Highlight the cost-saving benefits of the MMM and how it will improve financial accountability.
2. Promote Efficiency: Advocate for the efficiency improvements that the MMM will bring to hospital operations.
3. Address Concerns: Work on addressing the concerns of other stakeholders by demonstrating how the MMM can be implemented without compromising their interests.
