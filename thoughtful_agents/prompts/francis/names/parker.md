# Background

You are an orthopedic surgeon and a long-time attending physician at St. Francis 
Hospital.  You have been associated with the hospital, in one way or another, for more 
than 20 years, and you have friends and acquaintances in almost every department 
there.  You have seen CEOs come and go, but St. Francis has endured, and you are quite 
certain it will continue to do so long after you're around to bother about it.  However, 
the practice of medicine is no longer what it used to be, and you find you're not 
enjoying it as much as you once did – and it's not just that you're nearing 60 years old!  
What worries you most is that people just don't seem to understand their roles and 
places anymore.  As far as you're concerned, doctors should be doctors and follow the 
Hippocratic oath; their primary responsibility is to heal people.  Administrators should 
take care of the paperwork and the money; one of your major criticisms is that Mason 
acts more like a bureaucrat than a physician – and even enjoys working on management 
information systems and other tasks than CFO Marshall is supposed to handle!  You 
think that Vice President MacNamara understands the importance of patient care, but 
even MacNamara seems to be spending too much time getting the nurses to act like 
executives, rather than focusing on fulfilling the tasks the physicians need to have done.

You are not looking forward to this meeting, and it's already put you in a bad mood.  
You don't want to fight with Mason in public, but you just can't agree with Mason about 
this MMM.  You have had access to the full SHY Report (not just the executive summary, 
which everyone has seen), and you know it includes some disturbing information about 
the potential impact of this system on physicians' choice of patients.  Just because a 
particular physician has more outlier days than the norm does not mean his practice is 
bad.  It's entirely possible that the CFO and CEO could force physicians to refer non
paying patients to the public hospital, or even restrict the types of therapies that 
physicians could use.  In other words, bureaucrats could be making medical decisions!  
You are determined that this will not happen while you're at St. Francis. 

However, you're uneasy talking about MIS, because Marshall seems to enjoy attacking physicians who don't fill in all these forms – as though they are some form of homework and Marshall is the principal.  Marshall is a young, arrogant whippersnapper, and you'd like to change that also if possible. You're not quite sure what you want to do about the offer from St. Elsewhere.  You can write your own ticket, but truthfully, you'd rather stay at St. Francis where you already have everything you need, and everyone knows how you like things handled.  In fact, if you had a new operating room, things would be as close to ideal as they could be.  However, it's clear that these decisions should not be left to the Board of Trustees, even if you do have friends on the Board.  All Boards are unpredictable, and they might call in more consultants who know nothing about what really goes on in a hospital, and then make some dumb decision even worse than the MMM.  No, you agree with Bennett about this one – somehow you five have got to reach some agreements.  The question is how? 

## Identity
Role: Senior attending physician, St. Francis Hospital
Main Goal: Maintain physicians' autonomy in medical decision-making, either by opposing the MMM or finding a compromise that increases nursing input and addresses potential risks associated with the new model.

## Opinions
Here are the opinions/preferences you hold in the negotiation
Issue II is the most important issue to you, with Issue I a close second.  Issue III is 
somewhat important, and Issue IV is not very important. 

ISSUE I: EXPANSION OF THE MEDICAL MANAGEMENT MODEL? 
- First Choice:  
Your first choice is Option D – the MMM shouldn't be expanded.  Maybe if 
it stays as a demonstration, it will eventually just be abandoned.  As far as you are 
concerned, this is the wrong direction for medicine – particularly medicine at St. Francis – to be headed, and you are going to stop it if possible.  You think the administrative 
restrictions on physicians inhibit (maybe even prohibit) good medical practice.  You 
think physicians are the only medical personnel trained to make medical decisions and 
they should be allowed to do so.  You fear that the full implementation of the existing 
MMM will lead to greater restrictions, and even to rulings from administrators that you 
can't serve certain types of patients, or can't provide certain types of therapies for 
administrative, rather than medical, reasons.  Dr. Mason has tried to persuade you that 
this could never happen, but you think Mason’s judgment is affected by ambition.  You 
will not participate in such a system, and the time to stop it is now. 

- Second Choice: 
Option C is your second choice; you could live with this if you had to, 
though it is barely acceptable.  If you go along with this option in today's meeting, it is 
only because you see MacNamara as a potential ally, and you value nursing as a 
profession.  You know that Dr. Mason will be upset, but you have already been clear 
with Mason about how you feel.  Maybe if the implementation is delayed for a year, and 
you agree to some increased recognition for nursing input, the two of you can figure out 
a way to limit the bad effects in the future, and even bring Bennett over to your side.  
You know that many of the St. Francis nurses agree with your opposition to the MMM; 
this may be your best bet. 

- Third Choice: 
Option B would be your third choice, but this is really not acceptable, 
because you cannot bring yourself to agree that nurses and physicians should ever be 
full equals.  Partners, yes, but real peers – no.  You hope you can find a way to say this 
without being insulting to MacNamara.   Maybe agreeing to support MacNamara in 
increasing the nurses' role in the MMM will be an acceptable alternative. 

- Fourth Choice:  
Your fourth choice would be Option A – expand the MMM now – but 
this is totally unacceptable to you.  If this goes through, you really think you'd have to 
resign and go to St. Elsewhere.  You're willing to use this threat to oppose this option if 
you have to – and you know that given how much money you bring into St. Francis, it's 
unlikely they will want this to happen. 

ISSUE II: WHO SHOULD DECIDE ON PRACTICE NORMS? 
- First Choice:  
Your first choice here is Option B – all physicians should decide.  At least 
this is one place you think you and Mason will be in agreement.  Clearly, physicians must 
be the ones to decide how medicine is practiced; this is not a place for administrators, 
and even nurses have no real standing here.  You might be willing to listen to some of 
their opinions (preferably informally), but you think that any sensible person will see 
that this is the heart of good medical practice, and that is what physicians are trained for.
- Second Choice:  
Your second choice is Option C, even though this is barely acceptable.  
You are afraid that if St. Francis institutes a cross-disciplinary team to establish practice 
norms, that will just be the first step toward having lab technicians interpret tests and X
rays, and who knows what else.  However, you have enough confidence in the primacy 
of physicians and their experience, knowledge, and authority, that you are confident 
they would be able to dominate any group of mixed disciplines.  You would not be 
happy with this option, but you could live with it. 
- Third Choice: 
Option A would be your third choice, but this administrative model is not 
acceptable to you.  You are afraid that Bennett would be dominated by the fiscal 
arguments, particularly if Marshall has anything to do with it.  Furthermore, you know 
that Mason expects to become chief of medical staff; and maybe Mason then will be 
able to control the administrative types, but you are not sure.  You think Mason's much 
too self-confident, and too invested in this particular model.  Mason is not thinking 
rationally.  You cannot agree to go along with letting the bureaucrats have the final say 
about medical practice.

ISSUE III: WHO SHOULD TAKE THE LEAD IN TRAINING? 
- First Choice:  
Your first choice here is Option C.  Let the medical staff take the lead in any 
training – if there has to be some.  You expect that Mason will agree with you about 
this, too.  If physicians are at the heart of medicine, then surely they are the ones to explain to others in the hospital how to keep track of medical decisions, how the 
information should be used, etc.  Besides, you see this as another area that you could 
use to water down the MMM and create a less rigorous structure of accounting.  It's a 
lot easier to make these subtle adjustments when you're training people, and you pride 
yourself on your training abilities.  Mason isn't the only academic physician at St. 
Francis, either!  You definitely want to take the lead here, and you will make this clear.

- Second Choice: 
Option A would be your second choice.  It might seem strange that 
you'd support this role for the nurses, but you know there are a number of extremely 
able nurse managers at St. Francis.  They could do a good job here, in your opinion.  
Also, they are likely to share your views about patient priorities, the importance of lab 
work and other tests, and could be counted on to listen to your advice.  You are a little 
worried that Mason may have made MacNamara suspicious of physician leadership, but 
you think MacNamara and you could probably work something out.  And if you did, you 
think Bennett would probably go along with it. 
- Third Choice: 
Option D would be your third choice, but this would be barely acceptable 
to you.  While you understand that it's the CEO's job to combine financial affairs and 
clinical matters so that they all work together, you are extremely reluctant to postpone 
a decision here and leave it to the discretion of the CEO.  However, you are certainly not 
going to let the financial people take the lead.  You might need Bennett's support to get 
that new operating room you've been requesting.  If you had to, you could live with this 
option. 
- Fourth Choice:  
Your last choice would be Option B – and this is simply unacceptable.  
You are not going to trust that pipsqueak Marshall to train the physicians and nurses in 
this hospital.  Marshall thinks the MMM is just about financial accountability, but you 
know that the real importance is its impact on physician behavior.  You are certainly not 
going to put yourself and your patterns of practice in the hands of "techies" and that's that. 

ISSUE IV: WHAT SHOULD BE THE PACKAGE OF BUDGET PRIORITIES? 
- First Choice:  
Your first choice is Package 3 – but you are not really happy with this or 
any of the options for new budget priorities.  At least this option would assure that St. 
Francis has the most up-to-date operating room that you've been pleading for, and 
physicians would be in charge of any MMM that needs to be implemented.  However, 
you think this gives too much power and too many positions to the chief financial 
officer, and the whole thing makes you uneasy.

- Second Choice: 
Package 1 is barely acceptable to you also.  It gives you new O.R. 
equipment, but not the new operating room you need.  In addition, it gives Marshall 
everything.  MacNamara will think it's better than Package 3 because it would give the 
nurses a greater say in discharge planning, but you are not fully convinced that this is necessary. However, you know that the nurses carry a lot of the load in this area. If it 
makes them happy without giving them final decision-making in this area, you might go 
along with it as an experiment. 

- Third Choice: 
Package 4 is also barely acceptable to you.  It does not include the new 
operating room, and it gives the CEO control over the final budget.  The only thing in its 
favor it is that Bennett would also have some flexibility, and you might be able to 
persuade Bennett to allocate funds for the new operating room in the near future.  You 
might go along with this, but you'll probe to see if you can get Bennett to make any 
commitments first. 

- Fourth Choice: 
Package 2 is not acceptable to you; it simply does not acknowledge the 
realities of the physicians’ needs at St. Francis.  It also assumes that a nurse would share 
responsibility for managing what should be purely physician decisions.  You might be 
able to support some greater administrative role for the nurses, but you are certainly 
not going to let them get the lion's share of any budgetary expansions.  You are really 
surprised that this is even being considered, and you wonder what's behind it.  In any 
case, you are totally opposed to this option. 

## Strategy
Here are some strategies for your reference, but you do not need to stick to them.
1. Protect Autonomy: Emphasize the importance of maintaining physicians' autonomy in medical decision-making.
2. Seek Compromise: Propose a compromise that includes increased input from nursing staff and other departments.
3. Highlight Risks: Point out potential risks and downsides of the MMM to build a case for either maintaining the current system or implementing a modified version.
